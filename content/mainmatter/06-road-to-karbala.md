
# The Road to Karbalāʾ

<!-- TODO: Kūfans or Kufan -->

Initially, ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn Abī Bakr, ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿUmar, al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī,
and ʿAbdullāh ibn al-Zubayr \hon{\P_THM}{\BODY} were among those who did not
agree with the proposal that Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiyah should be designated as
successor during the lifetime of Muʿāwiyah \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. Before the
events of Karbalāʾ unfolded, both Muʿāwiyah and ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn Abī Bakr
\hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} had passed away. (_Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah_)

When Muʿāwiyah \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} died in Rajab of 60 AH, Yazīd assumed
authority and wrote to his governors, instructing them to secure the pledge of
allegiance from the leading figures of the Muslim community. The governor of
Madīnah at the time was al-Walīd ibn ʿUtbah, a man who had been involved in a
property dispute with al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} only months earlier. He was
further incited against al-Ḥusayn by Marwān ibn al-Ḥakam, who was known for
cursing ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and who had previously cursed him
from the pulpit during his tenure as governor of Madīnah.

Both al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī and ʿAbdullāh ibn al-Zubayr \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} were
summoned and ordered to pledge allegiance to Yazīd immediately. Marwān urged
al-Walīd to force them to pledge allegiance on the spot and, should they refuse,
to execute them. (_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_) However, neither complied. Al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} requested that the matter be discussed publicly rather than
in private and subsequently departed, and both were able to leave Madīnah and
make their way to Makkah.

Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} remained in Makkah for several months. During this
period, letters began arriving from Kūfah, in modern-day Iraq. The people of
Kūfah had a long history of support for the household of ʿAlī
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, and many of them were dissatisfied with Umayyad rule. Their
letters repeatedly invited al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} to come to Iraq,
promising him support and pledging allegiance to him should he arrive. It is
reported that their letters numbered over five hundred.

While in Makkah, it is reported that ʿAbdullāh ibn al-Zubayr \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}
visited Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and said, “I do not understand why these
people have abandoned us when we are the sons of the Muhājirūn and more
deserving of this affair than they are. Tell me, what do you intend to do?”

Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} replied, “By Allāh, I have been considering going
to Kūfah. My supporters there and their leading men have written to me. I
continue to seek guidance from Allāh.” Ibn al-Zubayr said, “If I possessed
supporters there like the supporters you have, I would never turn away from it.”

When Ibn al-Zubayr left, al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} remarked, “He knows that
he has no influence over this matter so long as I remain. The people have not
regarded him as they regard me. He wishes that I would depart so that the matter
may be left entirely to him.”

Several of the Ṣaḥābah and senior members of the Prophet's \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}
household attempted to dissuade al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} from
travelling to Kūfah. Among those who advised him against proceeding were
ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAbbās, ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿUmar, Muḥammad ibn al-Ḥanafīyah, and others
\hon{\P_THM}{\BODY}. They feared that the people of Kūfah would not remain loyal
once confronted by the military and political power of the Umayyad state. They
reminded him that the people of Kūfah had previously disappointed and abandoned
both his father ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and his brother al-Ḥasan
ibn ʿAlī \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. (_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_)

Among the most concerned was his half-brother Muḥammad ibn
al-Ḥanafiyyah[^muh_hanafiyah] \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. He urged al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} not to place his trust in the promises of the Kūfans and
advised him to remain in Makkah or seek refuge elsewhere until the political
situation became clearer. He feared that the pledges arriving from Kūfah would
prove unreliable and warned that the people might abandon him at the moment he
needed them most. (_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_)

    al-Ḥanafīyah. He was attributed to his mother so as to differentiate him

When Ibn ʿAbbās \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} realised that al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}
was determined to leave, he suggested that he travel to Yemen instead, where
there were many supporters of the household of ʿAlī \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. If he
insisted on going to Kūfah, Ibn ʿAbbās advised him at the very least not to take
the women and children of his family with him.

Ibn ʿAbbās \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} remained deeply concerned about the situation and
made repeated attempts to persuade al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} to reconsider.
He reminded him of the conduct of the people of Kūfah towards his father ʿAlī
and his brother al-Ḥasan \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} and feared that they would prove
just as unreliable once again.

Ṭāwūs \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} narrated that Ibn ʿAbbās \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} said,
“Al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī consulted me regarding his departure. I said, ‘Were it not
that it would bring disgrace upon both you and me, I would seize you by the head
and physically prevent you from leaving.’ The only response he gave me was,
‘That I be killed in such-and-such a place is more beloved to me than that I be
killed in Makkah.’ That statement was what ultimately caused me to resign myself
to his decision.” (_Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah_)

Abū Saʿīd al-Khudrī \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} also advised al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} against proceeding to Kūfah. He said, “O Abū ʿAbdillāh, I am
sincere towards you and concerned for you. News has reached me that people from
your supporters in Kūfah have written to you inviting you to go out to them, so
do not go to them. For I heard your father say in Kūfah, ‘By Allāh, I have grown
weary of them, and they have grown weary of me. I have come to dislike them, and
they have come to dislike me. I have found no loyalty among them. Whoever is
afflicted with them is afflicted with a broken arrow. They possess neither
firmness, nor resolve, nor patience in the face of the sword.’” (_Al-Ṭabaqāt
al-Kubrá_)

Seeking to assess the situation in Kūfah, al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}
dispatched his cousin Muslim ibn ʿAqīl ibn Abī Ṭālib \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} ahead
of him. Before departing from Makkah in 60 AH, Muslim was tasked with
investigating the sincerity of the people's support and reporting back on
conditions in the city.

Upon his arrival in Kūfah, Muslim ibn ʿAqīl \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} was initially
received with great enthusiasm. Large numbers of people pledged allegiance to
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} through him. He first stayed in the house of
al-Mukhtār ibn Abī ʿUbayd al-Thaqafī before later moving to the residence of
Hānī ibn ʿUrwah \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}. The people of Kūfah flocked to visit him,
renewing their promises of support and urging al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} to
come quickly. Some reports state that as many as eighteen thousand people
pledged allegiance to al-Ḥusayn through Muslim, while other reports place the
number even higher. Convinced that the people were sincere in their promises,
Muslim sent a letter to al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} informing him that the
situation appeared favorable and encouraging him to proceed towards Kūfah.
(_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_)

However, as news of Muslim ibn ʿAqīl's presence reached Yazīd, he removed Nuʿmān
ibn Bashīr \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} as governor of Kūfah and appointed a ruthless
twenty-six-year-old man named ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād, who was the governor of
Baṣrah at the time, in order to bring the situation under control.

Ibn Ziyād immediately began a campaign of intimidation and fear. The situation
quickly changed, and many of those who had pledged support to al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} became hesitant in the face of his threats and pressure.
Seeing the situation deteriorate, Muslim ibn ʿAqīl dispatched a second letter to
al-Ḥusayn, updating him on the turn of events and warning him of the dangers
that lay ahead. However, this warning would not reach al-Ḥusayn in time.

Upon entering Kūfah, Ibn Ziyād immediately began identifying the leaders of the
movement supporting al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. One of his most effective
tactics was the deployment of spies and informants throughout the city. Among
them was a man named Maʿqil, whom Ibn Ziyād provided with three thousand dirhams
and instructed to infiltrate the supporters of Muslim ibn ʿAqīl. Maʿqil
pretended to be a sympathizer of Ahl al-Bayt who wished to contribute money and
pledge his support to the cause. Through this deception, he gradually gained the
trust of Muslim's supporters and was eventually introduced to Muslim himself.

For several days, Maʿqil attended their gatherings, observed their activities,
and secretly reported everything back to Ibn Ziyād. Through him, Ibn Ziyād
discovered that Muslim was being sheltered in the house of Hānī ibn ʿUrwah
\hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}. Having obtained the information he needed, Ibn Ziyād
summoned Hānī ibn ʿUrwah to his palace. Initially, Hānī attempted to deny
knowledge of Muslim's whereabouts, but when confronted with the testimony of
Maʿqil, the deception was exposed. Ibn Ziyād demanded that Hānī hand Muslim
over. Hānī refused and insisted that he would never betray his guest. Ibn Ziyād
responded by striking him repeatedly with a cane until his face was badly
injured and then ordered him imprisoned. (_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_)

Upon hearing of Hānī's arrest, Muslim set out with his supporters towards the
residence of Ibn Ziyād. These so-called supporters numbered more than forty
thousand. Rather than confronting them militarily, Ibn Ziyād employed
psychological warfare. He ordered tribal leaders and influential figures to
address the people and warn them of the consequences of rebellion. They reminded
them of the approaching armies of Syria, threatened them with loss of stipends,
confiscation of wealth, destruction of property, and punishment of their
families. Fear spread rapidly through the crowd. Within a short time, their
numbers dwindled to around five thousand. Despite the rapidly shrinking ranks,
Muslim continued advancing towards Ibn Ziyād's residence.

To give the people a taste of his cruelty, Ibn Ziyād brought the captured Hānī
to the rooftop of his palace and threw him to his death. Witnessing this, many
more Kufans abandoned Muslim until fewer than five hundred remained around him.
Ibn Ziyād then renewed his threats, telling the people that they were now so few
in number that he could easily count them. Not only that, but he vowed to wipe
them out along with their families and homes. Hearing this, even more people
deserted Muslim until fewer than thirty supporters remained with him. The very
people who had written hundreds of letters to al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and
pledged their support had deserted his representative at the moment he needed
them most.

Imam Ibn Kathīr \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} records, “Muslim ibn ʿAqīl's supporters
started deserting him until their numbers fell to five hundred, then to three
hundred. Muslim eventually had only thirty men with him when he led the Maghrib
prayer and departed towards the gates of Kindah. By then only ten remained, and
before long they too abandoned him, leaving him alone. He wandered through the
streets of Kūfah in the darkness, unfamiliar with its roads and alleyways, not
knowing where to go or with whom to seek refuge.” (_Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah_)

They eventually took refuge in Masjid al-Anṣār. Ibn Ziyād ordered the roof of
the masjid to be torn open, and the remaining supporters were attacked. Muslim
was injured as he fled from the masjid. He sought refuge in the house of a woman
from the Kindah tribe. She gave him water to drink and granted him refuge for
the night. However, her son snitched and informed the authorities of Muslim's
whereabouts. Soldiers were dispatched, and after a fierce struggle in which
Muslim fought courageously despite being heavily outnumbered, he was captured
and brought before Ibn Ziyād by the very Kūfan people who had once pledged their
support to him.

After Muslim ibn ʿAqīl \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} was captured and brought before
ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād, he realized that his execution was imminent. Before being
taken to his death, he requested permission to make a final bequest. ʿUmar ibn
Saʿd ibn Abī Waqqāṣ was present, and Muslim said to him, “Between me and you is
kinship, so I have a need from you.” ʿUmar initially hesitated, but Ibn Ziyād
instructed him to hear what Muslim wished to say.

Muslim then took him aside and said, “I owe seven hundred dirhams in Kūfah which
were spent during my stay there. Sell my armor and repay my debt. Take my body
and ensure that it is buried. Then write to al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī and inform him of
what has happened, for I have written to him informing him that the people are
with him, and I fear that he may continue travelling towards Kūfah.”

ʿUmar ibn Saʿd conveyed these requests to Ibn Ziyād. Ibn Ziyād replied, “As for
his wealth, do with it as he instructed. As for his body, we have no need of it,
so let it be buried. As for al-Ḥusayn, if he does not seek to fight us, then we
will not fight him. But if he seeks to fight us, then we will fight him.”
(_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_; _Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah_)

Muslim ibn ʿAqīl was brutally executed publicly in Kūfah, and his body was
thrown from the top of a building, just as Hānī ibn ʿUrwah's had been, at the
command of Ibn Ziyād. This marked the beginning of a series of betrayals and
tragic events that would ultimately culminate in the Battle of Karbalāʾ.

When al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} prepared to depart from Makkah, ʿAbdullāh ibn
al-Zubayr \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} spoke with him near the Kaʿbah between al-Ḥajar
al-Aswad and the door of the Sacred House. He said, “If you wish to remain here,
then remain and take charge of this matter. We will support you, assist you,
advise you sincerely, and pledge allegiance to you.”

Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} replied, “My father informed me that there will be
a ram through whom the sanctity of Makkah will be violated, and I do not wish to
be that ram.” Ibn al-Zubayr then said, “Then remain if you wish, and appoint me
over the matter. You will be obeyed and not opposed.” Al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} replied, “Nor do I want that.” Thereafter, al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} performed _ṭawāf_ of the Kaʿbah, traversed between al-Ṣafā and
al-Marwah, shortened his hair, exited his _ʿumrah_, and then set out towards
Kūfah. (_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_)

Based upon the messages and letters arriving from Kūfah, together with the
initial letter of confirmation sent by Muslim ibn ʿAqīl, al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, at the age of fifty-six, set out towards Kūfah accompanied
by his family and a small group of loyal companions. His departure from Makkah
took place on the Day of Tarwiyah (8 Dhū al-Ḥijjah), just one day before the
killing of Muslim ibn ʿAqīl, for Muslim was killed on the Day of ʿArafah. He
began this journey unaware of the murder of Muslim ibn ʿAqīl and unaware of the
dramatic change in the attitude of the people of Kūfah. He proceeded only
because of their repeated invitations and because he did not wish to become the
cause of strife and bloodshed in Madīnah.

When news reached ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿUmar \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} that al-Ḥusayn ibn
ʿAlī \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} had set out towards Iraq, he hastened after him and
caught up with him three days into his journey. He asked, “Where do you intend
to go?” Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} replied, “To Iraq.” Ibn ʿUmar then noticed
the letters and documents that al-Ḥusayn was carrying and asked, “Are these the
letters and pledges of allegiance sent to you?” Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}
replied, “Yes.”

Thereupon Ibn ʿUmar \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} urged him not to continue and said, “Do
not go to them.” When al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} remained determined to
proceed, Ibn ʿUmar \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} said, “I will narrate to you something.
Jibrīl \hon{\S_HIM}{\BODY} came to the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} and gave him
the choice between this world and the Hereafter. The Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}
chose the Hereafter and did not choose this world. You are a part of the
Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}. By Allāh, no one among you will ever
attain worldly authority, and Allāh will not withhold it from you except that
what He has chosen for you is better.” Despite this sincere advice, al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} remained resolved to continue his journey. Realizing that he
would not turn back, Ibn ʿUmar \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} embraced him, wept, and bid
him farewell, saying, “I entrust you to Allāh and ask Him to protect you from
being killed.” (_Muʿjam Ibn al-Aʿrābī_)

Abū Wāqid al-Laythī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} also attempted to stop him. He said,
“When news reached me that al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī had departed, I caught up with him
at Malal. I adjured him by Allāh not to continue, for he was setting out upon a
course that was not the proper course of departure. Rather, he was going out to
his death.” Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} replied, “I will not return.”
(_Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah_)

Abū Bakr ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn al-Ḥārith ibn Hishām \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} also
met al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and said, “O cousin, kinship compels me to
advise you, and I do not know anyone more deserving of sincere counsel than you.
I have seen what the people of Iraq did to your father and your brother, yet you
now intend to travel to them. They are slaves of this world. Those who promise
to support you may well be the first to fight against you, while those who love
you may fail to aid you. I implore you by Allāh concerning yourself.” Al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} replied, “May Allāh reward you well, my cousin. Whatever
Allāh decrees shall come to pass.” Thereupon Abū Bakr said, “To Allāh we belong,
and to Him we shall return. We entrust Abū ʿAbdillāh to Allāh.” (_Al-Bidāyah wa
al-Nihāyah_)

Among the reports preserved regarding al-Ḥusayn's \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} journey to
Kūfah is his famous encounter with the renowned poet al-Farazdaq. When the two
met on the road, al-Farazdaq greeted him and said, “May Allāh grant you what you
hope for, and what you desire.” Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} then asked him,
“Tell me about the people and what lies behind you.”

Al-Farazdaq replied with words that would become one of the most famous
descriptions of the political climate surrounding Karbalāʾ, “The hearts of the
people are with you, but their swords are with Banū Umayyah. The decree descends
from Heaven, and Allāh does whatever He wills.”

Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} replied, “You have spoken the truth. The affair
belongs to Allāh. Allāh does whatever He wills. Every day our Lord is engaged in
a matter. If the decree descends in accordance with what we love, then we praise
Allāh for His blessings and seek His aid in showing gratitude. But if the decree
comes between us and our hopes, then the one whose intention is truth and whose
inner state is _taqwā_ has not failed.” Thereafter, al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}
set his mount in motion and said, “Peace be upon you.” The two then parted ways.
(_Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah_)

Had al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} intended to wage war, he would not have
travelled with the women and children of his household. It was only when he drew
near to Kūfah that news reached him of the murder of his cousin. At that point,
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} wished to return. However, the sons of Muslim ibn
ʿAqīl insisted on continuing and seeking justice for their father. Moved by
their words and emotions, al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} decided to proceed.

It is also interesting to note that Karbalāʾ lies on the route towards al-Shām,
where Yazīd was based, rather than in the direction of Kūfah, which had
originally been al-Ḥusayn's destination. This change in direction indicates that
he wished to meet Yazīd personally and settle the matter directly.

As al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} continued his journey towards Kūfah, ʿAbdullāh
ibn Jaʿfar \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} became deeply concerned for his safety. He wrote
to him saying, “I ask you by Allāh to return when you read this letter. I fear
that the course you have taken may lead to your destruction and the annihilation
of your household. If you are killed today, the light of the earth will be
extinguished. You are the banner of the guided and the hope of the believers. Do
not hasten in your journey, for I am sending this letter after you.” (_Tārīkh
al-Ṭabarī_)

Thereafter, ʿAbdullāh ibn Jaʿfar \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} approached ʿAmr ibn Saʿīd,
the governor of Makkah, and requested that he issue a written guarantee of
safety for al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and urge him to return. The governor
agreed and dispatched the letter with his brother Yaḥyá ibn Saʿīd. When they
caught up with al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and presented the letter to him, he
refused to return and said, “I saw the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} in
a dream, and he commanded me with a matter, and I am proceeding towards it.”
They asked him, “What was that vision?” Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} replied,
“I have not disclosed it to anyone, nor will I disclose it until I meet my
Lord.” (_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_)

As he travelled onwards, al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} dispatched Qays ibn
Mushir al-Ṣaydāwī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} to Kūfah carrying a letter to its people.
In it he wrote, “The letter of Muslim ibn ʿAqīl has reached me informing me of
your favorable opinion, the agreement of your leaders in supporting us, and
your desire to establish our right. We ask Allāh to grant us success and to
reward you with the greatest reward for that. I have departed from Makkah on
Tuesday, the eighth of Dhū al-Ḥijjah, the Day of Tarwiyah. When my messenger
reaches you, gather your affairs and prepare yourselves, for I am coming to you
in these coming days, if Allāh wills.” (_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_) 

Shortly thereafter, news finally reached al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} while
crossing the region of al-Qādisīyah, that Muslim ibn ʿAqīl and Hānī ibn ʿUrwah
\hon{\M_TWO}{\BODY} had been killed in Kūfah. Two travellers from Banū Asad
informed him, “By Allāh, we did not leave Kūfah until Muslim ibn ʿAqīl and Hānī
ibn ʿUrwah had been killed. We saw them being dragged through the marketplace by
their feet.” When al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} heard this, he repeatedly said,
“Indeed, we belong to Allāh, and to Him we shall return.”

Some of those accompanying al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} urged him to turn back.
Others argued that the people would rally behind him once he reached Kūfah.
However, members of Banū ʿAqīl declared, “By Allāh, we will not return until we
avenge our brother or taste what he tasted.” Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}
therefore continued his journey. (_Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah_)

A short while later, news reached al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} that Qays ibn
Mushir al-Ṣaydāwī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, the messenger whom he had dispatched to
Kūfah, had also been captured and killed by the authorities of Ibn Ziyād. With
the death of Muslim ibn ʿAqīl, Hānī ibn ʿUrwah, and now Qays ibn Mushir, it had
become increasingly apparent that the situation in Kūfah was very different from
what had been conveyed to him through the earlier letters and pledges.

Upon hearing this, al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} addressed those travelling with
him and said, “Our supporters have abandoned us. Whoever wishes to leave may do
so freely. There is no obligation upon him towards us.” Many of those who had
joined him along the road then departed, while those who remained did so knowing
the danger that now lay ahead. (_Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah_)

The journey therefore continued towards Iraq as the circle around al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} steadily tightened. The promises of Kūfah had vanished, the
leaders who had invited him had either been killed, imprisoned, or gone into
hiding, and the forces of Ibn Ziyād were now mobilizing to prevent him from
reaching the city.
