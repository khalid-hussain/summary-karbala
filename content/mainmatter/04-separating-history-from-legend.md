The events that unfolded upon the plains of Karbalāʾ continue to leave their
mark upon the Muslim Ummah to this very day. It is therefore essential to
distinguish the authentic historical record from the later embellishments,
exaggerations, and unauthenticated reports that remain widely circulated.

Among the most frequently cited sources concerning the events of Karbalāʾ is the
Kūfan historian Abū Mikhnaf Lūṭ ibn Yaḥyā (d. 157 AH / 774 CE). The scholars of
Ḥadīth did not regard him as a reliable authority in transmission and declared
him to be a liar. Imam Yaḥyá ibn Maʿīn said, “He is not trustworthy”. Imam Abū
Ḥātim al-Rāzī said, “His _ḥadīth_ is abandoned”. Imam al-Dāraquṭnī said, “Weak.”
Imam Ibn ʿAdī stated that he was a well-known Shīʿī and that the majority of his
narrations were not preserved. Imam al-Dhahabī described him as an _akhbārī_
(historian) who was not relied upon in transmission. Consequently, the detailed
reports attributed solely to Abū Mikhnaf regarding Karbalāʾ must be approached
with caution and measured against stronger and earlier evidence.

His reports contain numerous inconsistencies, are rife with exaggerations and
fabrications, and many of the detailed narratives attributed to him are
transmitted without verifiable chains. Despite passing away in 157 AH (774 CE),
nearly a century after the events of Karbalāʾ in 61 AH (680 CE), Abū Mikhnaf
claimed to possess numerous secret letters purportedly exchanged between
al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}, Muslim ibn ʿAqīl \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY},
Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiyah, ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād, and others.

Around eight centuries later, another work bearing the same title and attributed
to Abū Mikhnaf surfaced, its contents proving even more exaggerated and
fabricated than those previously ascribed to him. This later work was
subsequently relied upon by the Shia and, unfortunately, found its way into
some Sunni sources as well. The discussion of these matters serves as an
important reminder that historical inquiry, like every other branch of Islamic
knowledge, must be founded upon sound evidence, rigorous verification, and a
careful examination of sources.

At this point, an important question naturally arises. If Abū Mikhnaf was
criticized by the scholars of Ḥadīth, why do many of the major Sunni historians,
including al-Ṭabarī, Ibn al-Athīr, Ibn Kathīr, and others, continue to cite his
reports when discussing Karbalāʾ?

The answer lies in the distinction our scholars made between the science of
Ḥadīth and the science of history. The scholars of Ḥadīth were primarily
concerned with establishing matters of creed, law, and religious practice. For
these subjects, the strictest standards of verification were applied. Historical
narratives, however, were often treated differently. Historians frequently
preserved reports from a wide variety of transmitters, including individuals
whose narrations would not be accepted in matters of Ḥadīth. Their objective was
often to preserve what had been transmitted concerning an event rather than to
authenticate every detail contained within it.

Abū Mikhnaf occupies precisely this category. The leading scholars did not rely
on him as an authority in Ḥadīth, nor did they consider him trustworthy in the
manner of the great Ḥadīth transmitters. Yet they recognized that he was a Kūfan
historian who had access to local traditions, tribal accounts, names, locations,
and broad narratives concerning events that occurred in Iraq, particularly the
events surrounding Karbalāʾ.

For this reason, historians such as al-Ṭabarī transmitted many of his reports
while preserving their chains of transmission, thereby placing the
responsibility of evaluation upon later scholars and readers. Al-Ṭabarī himself
did not guarantee the authenticity of every report he recorded. Rather, he
compiled the available material and cited his sources.

This also explains why the scholars did not simply discard every report
transmitted by Abū Mikhnaf. Instead, they examined his narrations individually.
Reports that were corroborated by other evidence, supported by multiple routes,
or consistent with established historical facts were often accepted. Reports
that contained obvious exaggerations, sectarian embellishments, impossible
details, or contradictions with stronger evidence were rejected.

Consequently, the mere presence of Abū Mikhnaf in a chain of transmission does
not automatically render every historical detail false, nor does it render every
report authentic. His narrations must be weighed against the broader historical
record and judged on a case-by-case basis.

This is particularly important in the study of Karbalāʾ. The broad outline of
the tragedy is established beyond doubt through numerous independent sources.
The martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, the role of Ibn Ziyād, the
participation of ʿUmar ibn Saʿd and Shimr, the captivity of the Prophet's
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} family, and the subsequent events are all historically
established. The greatest caution is required not with the overall narrative
itself, but with the numerous speeches, conversations, private letters, dramatic
details, and emotional embellishments that appear in some later retellings.

The student of history must therefore avoid two extremes. One extreme is to
accept every report attributed to Abū Mikhnaf uncritically. The other is to
reject every report in which his name appears. The balanced approach followed by
the scholars of Ahl al-Sunnah is to distinguish between what is established,
what is plausible, and what is fabricated, accepting each report according to
the evidence supporting it.

Another important point must be noted. The tragedy of Karbalāʾ undoubtedly
occupies a significant place in Islamic history. However, no authentic evidence
establishes any special religious virtue for the land of Karbalāʾ itself. The
virtue belongs not to the soil, but to those righteous believers who were
betrayed, oppressed, and martyred there. It is these noble men and women,
foremost among them al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}, whose lives,
sacrifices, steadfastness, and adherence to Tawḥīd deserve our study,
reflection, and emulation. The true lesson of Karbalāʾ lies not in venerating a
location, but in following the path of faith, patience, courage, and devotion to
Allāh exemplified by those who gave their lives upon its plains.
