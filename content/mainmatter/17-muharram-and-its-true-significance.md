
# Muḥarram and Its True Significance

<!-- TODO: Muharram or al-Muharram -->

As we bring this work to a close, it is important to return to the true
significance of Muḥarram and the lessons that Allāh intended this blessed month
to teach us.

For many people, Muḥarram has become synonymous with Karbalāʾ alone. Yet
Muḥarram is far greater than a single historical event. Long before Karbalāʾ and
long before the martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, Allāh had already
made this month sacred and blessed.

The month of Muḥarram marks the beginning of the Hijrī year. It reminds us of
the Hijrah of our beloved Prophet Muḥammad \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} from Makkah to
Madīnah; a journey of sacrifice, perseverance, faith, and new beginnings. It
marks the transition from weakness to strength, from persecution to victory, and
from hardship to relief. It reminds us that every difficulty is followed by ease
and that the help of Allāh arrives exactly when He decrees it.

It also reminds us of the moment when Allāh helped Prophet Mūsá
\hon{\S_HIM}{\BODY} and Bani Israel while they were being pursued by Firʿawn
under the most impossible of circumstances. There was certain death before them
and certain death behind them. Hope appeared lost, and despair seemed to
surround them from every direction. Then the impossible happened, and the help
of Allāh arrived. The promise of Allāh is never false, nor is it ever late. It
comes with perfect wisdom and perfect timing. A path forward opened which none
could have imagined in their wildest dreams. Yet for Allāh it is as easy as:

\AYAH_AR_START
كُن فَيَكُونُ
\AYAH_AR_END

\AYAH_START
...Be, and it is. (Q2:117)
\AYAH_END

The Ṣaḥābah \hon{\P_THM}{\BODY} left everything they possessed and followed
their Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, just as Bani Israel followed Mūsá
\hon{\S_HIM}{\BODY}. What separated the eventual success of one group from the
destruction of the other was their adherence to the command of their Prophet.

The times may have changed, the people may have changed, and the Firʿawn of
every age may have changed, but Allāh remains the Most Powerful and Ever in
Command. If _tawakkul_ and complete trust and reliance upon Allāh can part the Red
Sea, then how great can our problems really be in comparison?

The Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} further elevated the status of this
month when he described it as, “The Month of Allāh, al-Muḥarram.” (_Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim_)
This attribution to Allāh highlights its special status, for Allāh only
attributes certain creations to Himself as a mark of honor and distinction. Just
as He attributed the Kaʿbah to Himself as Baytullāh (the House of Allāh), He
likewise attributed this month to Himself.

Linguistically, the word Muḥarram means “that which is made sacred” or
“forbidden.” It was given this name because fighting, bloodshed, aggression, and
warfare were prohibited during it, and it was counted among the sacred months
even before Islam.

## What is Muḥarram?

The blessed month of Muḥarram is the first month of the Hijrī calendar and one
of the four sacred months concerning which Allāh says,

\AYAH_AR_START
إِنَّ عِدَّةَ ٱلشُّهُورِ عِندَ ٱللَّهِ ٱثۡنَا عَشَرَ شَهۡرٗا فِي كِتَٰبِ ٱللَّهِ يَوۡمَ خَلَقَ ٱلسَّمَٰوَٰتِ وَٱلۡأَرۡضَ مِنۡهَآ أَرۡبَعَةٌ حُرُمٞۚ ذَٰلِكَ ٱلدِّينُ ٱلۡقَيِّمُۚ فَلَا تَظۡلِمُواْ فِيهِنَّ أَنفُسَكُمۡۚ
\AYAH_AR_END

\AYAH_START
Indeed, the number of months with Allāh is twelve months in the decree of Allāh
from the day He created the heavens and the earth. Of these, four are sacred.
That is the correct religion, so do not wrong yourselves during them.
(Al-Tawbah, Q9:36)
\AYAH_END

Ibn ʿAbbās \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} said that the phrase, “So do not wrong yourselves
during them,” refers to all the months of the year. However, these four months
were singled out and made sacred, such that sins committed during them are more
serious and righteous deeds carry a greater reward.

Just like when there is a major sale, or when the latest iPhone is launched,
people go absolutely crazy. They stand in line for hours on end and spend enough
money on a single phone that could feed entire families. We are not saying that
this is wrong or that people should not enjoy the blessings that Allāh has
bestowed upon them.

The point I am trying to get across is that Muḥarram is a gift and a blessing
from Allāh. It is a month in which we should strive to accumulate as many
rewards as possible. We should take full advantage of Allāh's mercy and
generosity, for good deeds in these blessed times are multiplied by His will.

When we increase the degree of our worship, Allāh increases His rewards for us.
This is one promotion that I can confidently say does not come with any hidden
conditions or marketing gimmicks. If we enter this month sincerely seeking the
pleasure of Allāh, the rewards will be magnified to numbers beyond our
imagination. This is one offer that we simply cannot afford to miss.

The beautiful part is that if you tell others about this opportunity and they
act upon it, then whatever good they do, you also receive a reward similar to
theirs without their reward being diminished in the slightest. How amazing is
that? The Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, “Whoever guides someone to goodness
will have a reward like one who did it.” (_Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim_)

At the same time, we must be extra careful to avoid sins, because just as the
rewards of good deeds are increased, the severity of wrongdoing is also greater
in these sacred months.

We see the wisdom behind the selection of these sacred months in the words of
Imam al-ʿIzz ibn ʿAbd al-Salām \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, who said that Allāh
designates certain times as sacred and blessed in order to encourage His
servants towards righteous deeds and to bestow greater rewards upon them.
(_Qawāʿid al-Aḥkām_) Such is our Rabb, the Most Merciful, the Most High.

We know that the Hijrī calendar is tied to the Hijrah, but how did Muḥarram
become the first month of the year when the Hijrah itself took place in Rabīʿ
al-Awwal?

## The Hijrī Calendar

The most famous calendars employed by the nations of the world were the Julian
and Gregorian calendars. In 46 BC, Julius Caesar chose the 1st of January as the
first day of the New Year, as Janus symbolically represented the doorway into
the new year. The Gregorian calendar was later introduced in October 1582 by
Pope Gregory XIII as a modification of the Julian calendar.

Scholars such as Imam Ibn Ḥajar, Imam al-Ḥākim, and Imam Ibn Kathīr, among many
others, have recorded the origins of the Islamic calendar.

At the time of the Ṣaḥābah \hon{\P_THM}{\BODY}, multiple calendars were in use
simultaneously. The Sassanids in Persia used a calendar beginning from the
accession of Yazdegerd III on 16 June 632 CE. The regions of Syria under
Byzantine rule used a form of the Roman Julian calendar, while the people of
Egypt used the Coptic calendar, which began on 29 August 284 CE.

Scholars such as Imam al-Zuhrī and Imam al-Ṭabarī record that when the Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} arrived in Madīnah during Rabīʿ al-Awwal, he instructed that
correspondence and calculations be referenced accordingly. This indicates that
the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} had already established an organized system for
recording matters and keeping track of dates.

What is authentically established is that the idea of formally counting and
numbering the years was first raised by ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}
due to the increasing volume of correspondence reaching his administration. A
standardized dating system became necessary so that dates of writing and
dispatch could be properly identified.

Two key incidents prompted this action.

The first was a dispute concerning a loan repayment schedule presented before
ʿUmar \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. One man said, “O Amīr al-Muʾminīn, this man was
supposed to repay me by Shaʿbān, and it is already Ramaḍān.”

The other replied, “When I said Shaʿbān, I meant the Shaʿbān of the following
year.”

The second was a letter sent by Abū Mūsá al-Ashʿarī \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY},
governor of Baṣrah, in which he wrote, “O Amīr al-Muʾminīn, sometimes your
instructions reach us specifying a particular month, yet we do not know whether
you mean the current year or the following year.”

This occurred approximately in the fourth year of ʿUmar's caliphate, in 17 AH
(638 CE).

It is important to note that the Hijrī calendar is a lunar calendar, meaning
that it is based upon the cycle of the moon and the sighting of the new
crescent. As a result, the number of days in each month can only be either 29 or
30 days. No month in the Hijrī calendar contains 28 days or 31 days.

Another important point to note is that a new day in the Islamic calendar begins
at sunset and not after midnight. Thus, in Islam, the night precedes the day.

Allāh says,

\AYAH_AR_START
هُوَ ٱلَّذِي جَعَلَ ٱلشَّمۡسَ ضِيَآءٗ وَٱلۡقَمَرَ نُورٗا وَقَدَّرَهُۥ مَنَازِلَ لِتَعۡلَمُواْ عَدَدَ ٱلسِّنِينَ وَٱلۡحِسَابَۚ مَا خَلَقَ ٱللَّهُ ذَٰلِكَ إِلَّا بِٱلۡحَقِّۚ يُفَصِّلُ ٱلۡأٓيَٰتِ لِقَوۡمٖ يَعۡلَمُونَ ٥
\AYAH_AR_END

\AYAH_START
It is He who made the sun a shining light and the moon a reflected light, and
determined for it phases so that you may know the number of years and the
reckoning of time. Allāh did not create this except in truth. He explains the
signs in detail for a people who possess knowledge. (Yūnus, Q10:5)
\AYAH_END

The moon therefore serves not only as a sign from the signs of Allāh, but also
as the means by which the Muslim measures time, organizes his worship, and marks
the passing of months and years.

## What Should We Do in Muḥarram?

### 1. Fasting {.unnumbered}

Although the month of Muḥarram is sacred in its entirety, the 10th day of
Muḥarram occupies a special distinction amongst its days. This day is known as
ʿĀshūrāʾ.

Imam al-Suyūṭī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} said, “I was asked why Muḥarram is singled
out as ‘the Month of Allāh’ from the rest of the months, although there are
months that possess equal or even greater virtue, such as Ramaḍān. I found the
following answer: Muḥarram is an Islamic name, unlike the names of the other
months, which were already known during the Jāhiliyyah. During the Jāhiliyyah it
was known as Ṣafar al-Awwal, followed by Ṣafar al-Thānī. When Islam came, Allāh
named it al-Muḥarram, so it was attributed to Allāh because of this
distinction.” (_Sharḥ Muslim_)

In a month beloved to Allāh, our beloved Messenger \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} gave us
yet another glad tiding.

Abū Qatādah \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} reported that the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} was asked about fasting on the day of ʿĀshūrāʾ, and he
replied, “It expiates the sins of the previous year.” (_Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim_)

Things just seem to get better and better, do they not? Up until now, we have
seen that righteous deeds are multiplied during these sacred times. Then Allāh,
the Most Generous, informed us through His Messenger \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} that
fasting on this day can erase an entire year's worth of minor sins.

Our teacher, Shaykh Bilal Ismail \hon{\H_HIM}{\BODY}, highlights an important
point here. It is not a given that everyone who fasts will automatically be
forgiven. Rather, it depends upon Allāh accepting that fast. Therefore, we
should ask Allāh not only for the _tawfīq_ (ability) to fast, but also for the
acceptance of our fasting.

#### The Development of Fasting ʿĀshūrāʾ {.unnumbered}

The fasting of ʿĀshūrāʾ passed through several stages during the lifetime of the
Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}.

ʿĀʾishah \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} said, “The Quraysh used to fast on the day of
ʿĀshūrāʾ in the pre-Islamic period. The Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}
would also fast on it during the pre-Islamic period.” (_Sunan Abī Dāwūd_)

This shows that the sanctity of the day was already known amongst the Arabs, and
remnants of the teachings of Ibrāhīm or Nūḥ \hon{S_TWO}{\BODY} may have reached
them. The Quraysh would even change the covering of the Kaʿbah on the day of
ʿĀshūrāʾ.

When the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} migrated to Madīnah, he found the Jews
fasting on the same day. Ibn ʿAbbās \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} narrated, “The Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} came to Madīnah and saw the Jews fasting on the day of
ʿĀshūrāʾ. He asked them about it. They replied, ‘This is a righteous day. It is
the day on which Allāh saved Bani Israel from their enemy, so Mūsá fasted on
it.’ The Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, ‘We have more right to Mūsá than you.’
So he fasted on that day and commanded the Muslims to fast it.” (_Ṣaḥīḥ
al-Bukhārī_)

During this period, fasting ʿĀshūrāʾ was obligatory. The Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} would even send callers throughout Madīnah instructing the
people to fast.

Salamah ibn al-Akwaʿ \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} narrated, “The Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} ordered a man on the day of ʿĀshūrāʾ to announce, ‘Whoever
has eaten should not eat any more for the rest of the day, and whoever has not
eaten should complete his fast.’” (Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī)

However, when fasting Ramaḍān was made obligatory in the second year after
Hijrah, the obligation of fasting ʿĀshūrāʾ was lifted, and it became a highly
recommended Sunnah.

ʿĀʾishah \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} reported, “When fasting Ramaḍān was made
obligatory, the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, ‘Whoever wishes may
fast ʿĀshūrāʾ and whoever wishes may leave it.’” (_Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim_)

Likewise, ʿAlqamah \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} reported that Ashʿath ibn Qays visited
Ibn Masʿūd \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} while he was eating on the day of ʿĀshūrāʾ. He
remarked, “It is the day of ʿĀshūrāʾ.” Ibn Masʿūd replied, “Fasting on this day
was observed before Ramaḍān was made obligatory. When Ramaḍān became obligatory,
it was left as a voluntary fast.” (_Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim_)

#### How Should We Fast ʿĀshūrāʾ? {.unnumbered}

Towards the end of his life, the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} desired
that the Muslims distinguish themselves from the exclusive practice of the Jews
by fasting an additional day alongside the tenth.

Ibn ʿAbbās \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} reported that the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, “If I remain alive until the next year, I will
certainly fast the ninth.” (_Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim_)

In another narration, Ibn ʿAbbās \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} said, “When the Messenger
of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} fasted on ʿĀshūrāʾ and commanded the Muslims to
fast, they said, ‘O Messenger of Allāh, it is a day that is venerated by the
Jews and Christians.’ The Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} replied, ‘If I
live until next year, _in-shāʾ Allāh_, we will fast the ninth as well.’ But the
Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} passed away before the next year arrived.”
(_Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim_)

It is therefore recommended to fast the ninth along with the tenth of Muḥarram
in order to distinguish ourselves from the practice of the Jews.

Shaykh Bilal Ismail \hon{\H_HIM}{\BODY} elaborates on this point and says, “We
have our own identity, values, and beliefs. There is no harm in standing out;
rather, it is something to be proud of. We do not blindly follow whatever others
practice. A small action can earn a mighty reward from Allāh. Allāh wants to
give us the opportunity to change. It is up to us to take that opportunity.”

Abū ʿUthmān al-Nahdī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} said, “The pious predecessors and
successors used to venerate three sets of ten: the first ten days of Muḥarram,
the first ten days of Dhū al-Ḥijjah, and the last ten days of Ramaḍān.”
(_Al-Durr al-Manthūr_)

Imam Ibn al-Qayyim \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} explained, “There are three levels of
fasting ʿĀshūrāʾ. The most complete is to fast a day before it and a day after
it. After this is to fast the ninth and the tenth, and the majority of the
narrations support this. Lastly is to fast the tenth by itself.” (_Zād
al-Maʿād_)

Likewise, Imam Ibn Ḥajar al-ʿAsqalānī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} mentioned in _Fatḥ
al-Bārī_, “Fasting ʿĀshūrāʾ is of three levels:

1. To fast the tenth alone.
2. To fast the ninth along with it.
3. To fast the ninth and the eleventh along with it.

And Allāh knows best.”

Some people raise concerns regarding fasting the eleventh of Muḥarram. Certain
scholars have graded the narrations concerning fasting the eleventh as weak,
whilst others have considered them _ḥasan_ (fair). Beyond the narrations
themselves, fasting the eleventh serves the purpose of further distinguishing
the Muslim from the fasting of the Jews and is also in harmony with the
Prophetic encouragement to fast during Muḥarram.

The scholars also mentioned another reason for fasting the eleventh: to ensure
that one does not miss the tenth due to an error in moon-sighting. If a Muslim
fasts the ninth, tenth, and eleventh, then he has undoubtedly fasted ʿĀshūrāʾ.

Imam Ibn Abī Shaybah narrated in al-Muṣannaf from Ṭāwūs \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} that
he used to fast a day before and a day after ʿĀshūrāʾ out of fear of missing it.

Imam Aḥmad \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} said, “Whoever wishes to fast ʿĀshūrāʾ should
fast the ninth and the tenth, unless there is uncertainty regarding the month,
in which case he should fast three days.” He then added, “Ibn Sīrīn held this
view as well.” (_Al-Mughnī_)

Fasting is one of those acts of worship that are especially beloved to Allāh. It
is an act regarding which Allāh did not specify a fixed reward, but instead said
that He Himself would reward it. We can let our imagination run as far as it
can, yet still fail to comprehend the magnitude of what Allāh has prepared for
those who fast sincerely seeking His pleasure.

### 2. Give Charity {.unnumbered}

Giving charity is one of the best things we can do during this blessed month. As
we know, every righteous deed performed during these sacred times is magnified
by the permission of Allāh.

Abū Hurayrah \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} reported that the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, “None gives _ṣadaqah_ from what is good---for Allāh
only accepts what is good---except that the Merciful takes it with his right
hand. Even if it is a date, it is nurtured in the palm of the Merciful until it
becomes greater than a mountain, just as one of you nurtures his young horse or
camel.” (_Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim_)

Imam Ibn al-Qayyim \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} stated, “If those who give _ṣadaqah_ knew
that their charity falls into the Hand of Allāh before it reaches the hand of
the poor person, then the joy of giving would be greater than the joy of
receiving.” (_Madārij al-Sālikīn_)

So why pass up such an opportunity? This is an investment that can only bring
profit. Go ahead, do not hold back, and give charity generously for the sake of
Allāh.

The ʿUlamāʾ of the Ḥanābilah mention that people should be generous towards
their families and dependents during the month of Muḥarram. It is narrated from
ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAmr ibn al-ʿĀṣ \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} that he said, “Whoever fasts
on the tenth of Muḥarram, it is as though he fasted the entire year, and whoever
gives charity on that day, it is like the charity of an entire year.” (_Laṭāʾif
al-Maʿārif_)

The Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} is also reported to have said,
“Whoever spends generously on his family on the day of ʿĀshūrāʾ, Allāh will be
generous to him for the remainder of the year.” (_Shuʿab al-Īmān_)

Ibn Manṣūr asked Imam Aḥmad \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, “Do you know of a _ḥadīth_ that
says, ‘Whoever spends generously on his family on the day of ʿĀshūrāʾ, Allāh
will be generous to him for the entire year.’?”

He replied, “Yes. It was narrated by Sufyān ibn ʿUyaynah from Jaʿfar al-Aḥmar
from Ibrāhīm ibn Muḥammad ibn al-Muntashir---who was one of the best people of
his time---that it had reached him: ‘Whoever spends generously on his family on
the day of ʿĀshūrāʾ, Allāh will be generous to him for the entire year.’”

Sufyān ibn ʿUyaynah \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} then remarked, “I have practiced this
for fifty or sixty years and have found nothing but good from it.”

It is also reported from Jābir \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} that he said, “We practiced
this and found it to be true.”

Abū al-Zubayr and Shuʿbah \hon{\M_TWO}{\BODY} are likewise reported to have said
similar words.

It should be noted, however, that there is a difference of opinion regarding the
authenticity of the chains of transmission for these narrations. Many scholars
regarded them as weak, including Imam Aḥmad, Imam al-Dāraquṭnī, Imam Ibn
al-Qayyim, Shaykh al-Islām Ibn Taymīyah, Imam al-Ṭabarānī, and Shaykh al-Albānī
\hon{\M_THM}{\BODY}.

Shaykh al-Islām Ibn Taymīyah \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} said, “One of the famous
_athar_ comes through the narration of Ibrāhīm ibn Muḥammad al-Muntashir from
his father who said, ‘It has reached us that...’ This wording is disconnected,
and it is unknown from whom it was received. It was most likely fabricated when
conflict arose between the Rāfiḍah and the Nawāṣib. When the Rāfiḍah turned the
day of ʿĀshūrāʾ into a day of mourning, the Nawāṣib introduced reports
encouraging people to spend on themselves and their families and to treat it as
a day of celebration. Both of these approaches are false.” (_Iqtiḍāʾ al-Ṣirāṭ
al-Mustaqīm_)

<!-- (_Iqtiḍāʾ al-Ṣirāṭ al-Mustaqīm_, 2/132) -->

On the other hand, these narrations were strengthened or accepted by a number of
scholars. Imam al-Suyūṭī authenticated them, Imam al-Kurdī graded them _ḥasan_ in
_al-Ḥawāshī al-Madanīyah_, Imam al-Bayhaqī strengthened them due to the
multiplicity of their chains, and both Imam al-Ḥaṣkafī and Imam Ibn ʿĀbidīn
accepted them in their respective works.

Shaykh al-Islām Ibn Taymīyah \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} further stated, “Nothing is
legislated on ʿĀshūrāʾ except fasting. As for what has been narrated concerning
applying kohl, dyeing the hair, performing ghusl specifically for the day,
praying a special prayer, or spending more on one's family due to the day
itself, then all of these reports are fabricated according to the scholars of
_Ḥadīth_, despite their widespread circulation among the people.” (_Al-Radd ʿalā
al-Shādhilī_)

At the end of the day, it is best not to depend on these narrations nor to
actively propagate them. As for charity in general, there is no disagreement
whatsoever regarding its virtue, especially during the sacred months. So give
generously, help those in need, support Islamic causes, and spend on your
family seeking the pleasure of Allāh.

### 3. Dhikr {.unnumbered}

By now we already know how immense the rewards of _dhikr_ are. Performing _dhikr_
during these sacred days simply makes the rewards even greater. Just keep the
multiplication factor in mind, and you will find yourself motivated to increase
in the remembrance of Allāh.

This has to be one of the easiest ways of earning rewards. We can do it
anywhere, anytime, and in almost any situation. Let us look at some amazing
opportunities:

The Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, “Whoever says:

\HADITH_AR_END

### 4. Visit the Sick and the Orphans {.unnumbered}

This is one of the greatest _īmān_ boosters a person can experience. Visiting the
sick and caring for orphans reminds us of the countless blessings that Allāh has
bestowed upon us.

Having good health, a stable family, and loved ones around us are blessings that
many people take for granted until they are tested with their absence.

### 5. Uphold Family Ties {.unnumbered}

This might be difficult for some people, but you are rewarded even for making
that phone call to an uncle you have been avoiding. Use this month to strengthen
ties of kinship, honor your parents, and ask them to make _duʿāʾ_ for you. The
_duʿāʾ_ of righteous parents is a blessing that many people no longer have the
opportunity to enjoy. Cherish it while you can. Allāh loves those who are
dutiful to their parents, and when the parents are pleased, the pleasure of
Allāh follows.

### 6. Reading the Quran {.unnumbered}

From the perspective of rewards, reading the Quran is among the greatest
investments a person can make. For every letter recited, ten rewards are
recorded. Then Allāh multiplies those rewards further from His bounty however He
wills.

Trying to calculate the total reward is ultimately pointless because no one can
place a limit on the generosity of Allāh. _Iqraʾ_! Read!

The Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, “Whoever recites a letter from
the Book of Allāh will receive one good deed, and that good deed will be
multiplied by ten. I do not say that _Alif Lām Mīm_ is one letter; rather _Alif_
is a letter, _Lām_ is a letter, and _Mīm_ is a letter.” (_Jāmiʿ al-Tirmidhī_)

### 7. Revive the Sunnah {.unnumbered}

Try to perform as many Sunnah acts as possible. Nothing is more beloved than
following the example of the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} and imitating
him in seeking the pleasure of Allāh. There is a reason Allāh sent him to us and
described him as the best example for mankind.

Another important aspect of reviving the Sunnah is teaching the
people---especially children and teenagers---the authentic Sīrah of the Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} and the history of this Ummah. Many people shy away from
discussing events such as the Battles of al-Jamal and Ṣiffīn, the events of
Karbalāʾ, the martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, and the virtues of Ahl
al-Bayt \hon{\P_THM}{\BODY}. This approach can have serious long-term
consequences.

If our children do not learn these matters from balanced scholars grounded in
the Sunnah, they will eventually hear distorted versions from the Shia and other
misguided groups, leading to confusion, doubts, and misconceptions about the
history of Islam.
