
# 10th of Muḥarram: A Day of Mourning?

The Shia have transformed the blessed month of Muḥarram from a season of
worship, gratitude, and remembrance of Allāh into a season of mourning and
lamentation centred upon the martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}.

Many people have come to believe that Muḥarram is a month in which marriages and
other celebrations are impermissible because it is supposedly a period of
mourning. This belief is entirely baseless and constitutes an innovation in the
religion.

A fundamental principle must be understood here: if the Sharīʿah has not singled
out a day as a day of religious mourning, then no historical event can transform
it into one. There is no day of mourning or evil in the Sharīʿah of Muḥammad
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}. Likewise, if the Sharīʿah has not designated a day as a
religious festival (Eid), then no historical event can transform it into a day
of celebration. No birth, death, victory, or tragedy has the authority to
introduce a new religious observance into Islam.

The pages of Islamic history are filled with the blood of martyrs. If we were to
begin mourning the martyrdom of the Ṣaḥābah alone, every other day would become
a day of mourning. Therefore, attributing the significance of ʿĀshūrāʾ to the
martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} is baseless. In reality, it was a
blessing from Allāh that He granted al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} martyrdom
during this blessed month. Thus, the host of customs, ceremonies, and rituals
later attached to his martyrdom must be rejected.

Shaykh al-Islām Ibn Taymīyah \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} commented, “Since al-Ḥasan and
al-Ḥusayn are the leaders of the youth of Jannah, and were born after the Hijrah
during the period of the dominance of Islam, they did not experience many of the
hardships endured by those righteous individuals who preceded them. Therefore,
Allāh bestowed upon them favors through trials and tribulations so that their
ranks might be elevated. This was from their honor and virtue, not from any
disgrace, just as Allāh honored Ḥamzah, Jaʿfar, ʿUmar, ʿUthmān, ʿAlī and others
through martyrdom.” (_Raʾs al-Ḥusayn_)

There are two extremes, and both are to be rejected. One side turns Muḥarram
into a season of mourning and lamentation, while the other turns it into a
season of widespread celebration. The Ummah remains upon the middle path and
exaggerates in neither direction.

If martyrdom were to be commemorated through annual mourning, then many of the
Prophets of Allāh were killed by their people. Ḥamzah ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} was mutilated and martyred. ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} was assassinated in Muḥarram. ʿUthmān ibn ʿAffān
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} was brutally murdered while reciting the Quran. ʿAlī ibn Abī
Ṭālib \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} was assassinated. Likewise, many other Ṣaḥābah whose
virtues and ranks are well known were martyred. Yet we do not find the Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, the Ṣaḥābah, or the early generations establishing annual
commemorations, mourning ceremonies, or ritual gatherings connected to their
deaths.

Look also at the inconsistency of the Shia themselves. ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} was murdered unjustly, and al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}
lived for approximately twenty years after his father's assassination. Yet
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} never established annual mourning gatherings,
remembrance ceremonies, or lamentations for his father. Nor did the family of
ʿAlī \hon{\P_THM}{\BODY} do so. Even today, the Shia do not devote to ʿAlī
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} what they devote to al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, despite
the fact that ʿAlī \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} was superior to his son.

We must remember the teachings of our Prophet \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, who even
taught us the etiquette of mourning. Umm Ḥabībah and Zaynab bint Jaḥsh
\hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} narrated that the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, “It is
not lawful for a woman who believes in Allāh and the Last Day to mourn for more
than three days, except for her husband, for whom she mourns four months and ten
days.” (_Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī_)

Shaykh Dr. Tawfique Chowdhury \hon{\H_HIM}{\BODY} once asked a Shia adherent,
“Why do you beat yourselves and mourn?” The man replied that they mourned over
the death of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. The Shaykh then asked, “Why do you
mourn when, without a doubt, al-Ḥusayn is in Jannah? Not only is he in Jannah,
but he is among the leaders of its youth, exactly as the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} informed us. Both Sunnis and Shia agree that al-Ḥusayn is
from the people of Jannah.”

The believer grieves the injustice that befell al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY},
but he also rejoices in the immense honor that Allāh bestowed upon him. His
martyrdom was a tragedy, but his final destination was Jannah. This does not
mean that we are indifferent towards those who murdered him. Rather, we believe
that whoever participated in his killing, assisted in it, or was pleased with it
has committed a tremendous crime.

ʿUrs ibn ʿUmayrah al-Kindī \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} narrated that the Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, “If a sin is committed on earth, the one who witnesses
it and hates it is like the one who was absent from it, and the one who was
absent from it but is pleased with it is like the one who witnessed it.” (_Sunan
Abī Dāwūd_)

This proves that if a person is pleased with evil, he shares in its sin even if
he was not physically present. Therefore, whoever is pleased with the killing of
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} is sinful regardless of the era in which he lived.

Shaykh al-Islām Ibn Taymīyah \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} said, “As for the one who
killed al-Ḥusayn, assisted in his killing, or was pleased with that, then upon
him is the curse of Allāh, the angels, and all mankind. Allāh will not accept
from him any obligatory or voluntary deed.” (_Majmūʿ al-Fatāwá_)

While Ahl al-Sunnah reject the innovations of mourning and lamentation
associated with Karbalāʾ, this does not mean that the day of ʿĀshūrāʾ itself
possesses no virtue. On the contrary, its virtue was established by the
Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} long before the martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}.

One of the most common claims made by the Shia is that fasting on the 10th of
Muḥarram is an Umayyad innovation introduced to overshadow the memory of
Karbalāʾ. As with many of their claims, this allegation is contradicted not only
by authentic Sunni narrations but even by reports found within their own books.

Al-Majlisī narrates, “Imam al-Ḥusayn \hon{\S_HIM}{\BODY} said on the night of
the tenth of Muḥarram, ‘I saw my grandfather, the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, in a dream. He said to me, “My son, you will soon join me.
Let your breaking of the fast be with me tonight. Hasten and do not delay.”’”
(_Bihār al-Anwār_)

Grand Āyatullāh Abū al-Qāsim al-Khūʾī stated, “The narrations that prohibit
fasting on this day do not possess authentic chains of transmission; rather, all
of them are weak. In contrast, there are numerous authentic narrations regarding
the virtue of fasting on this day and encouraging it. For example, it is
reported, ‘Fasting on the day of ʿĀshūrāʾ expiates the sins of a year.’
Likewise, another narration states, ‘Fast on the ninth and the tenth of
Muḥarram, for doing so expiates the sins of an entire year.’ Therefore, the
strongest opinion is that fasting on this day remains recommended and virtuous.”
(_Kitāb al-Ṣawm_)
