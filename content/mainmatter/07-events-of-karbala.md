
# The Events of Karbalāʾ

Al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}, accompanied by his family members and a
small group of loyal followers, arrived at the plains of Karbalāʾ, situated near
the Euphrates River in present-day Iraq, on the 2nd of Muḥarram, 61 AH (680 CE).
As al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} continued his journey, he eventually reached
the plain upon which his final stand would take place. Upon arriving, he asked
those with him, “What is the name of this land?” They replied, “Karbalāʾ.” Upon
hearing its name, al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} reflected upon it and said,

كَرْبٌ وَبَلَاءٌ
\HADITH_AR_END

\HADITH_START
“Distress and tribulation.”
\HADITH_END

According to some reports, he then recognized that this was the very land
concerning which he had previously heard mention and understood that a great
trial awaited him there. (_Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah_)

Before reaching Karbalāʾ, he encountered a force of approximately one thousand
horsemen under the command of al-Ḥurr ibn Yazīd al-Tamīmī, who had been
dispatched by ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād to intercept him and prevent him from
entering Kūfah. Al-Ḥurr was not initially sent to fight al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. Rather, his instructions were to monitor his movements and
prevent him from reaching Kūfah until further orders arrived from Ibn Ziyād.
During this encounter, al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} addressed the men before
him and reminded them of the hundreds of letters and invitations that had been
sent to him from Kūfah. He made clear that he had not come seeking authority for
himself but had responded to the repeated requests of the people who had invited
him.

It is noteworthy that ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād had entered Kūfah with only a
relatively small contingent. The overwhelming majority of those who would
eventually form the army opposing al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} came from the
people of Kūfah themselves. Historians across the various schools of Islamic
history agree that the ranks of the army were filled largely by the very Kūfans
who had previously written to al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, pledged allegiance
to him, and urged him to come to their city.

As negotiations continued, additional troops arrived from Kūfah. What had begun
as a force of roughly one thousand men gradually grew into a much larger army.
Eventually, command was entrusted to ʿUmar ibn Saʿd ibn Abī Waqqāṣ, who arrived
with a force reported to number around four thousand men. He confronted
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and conveyed the demand of Ibn Ziyād that he
submit to his authority. Thus began the final chapter of one of the most painful
and consequential events in Islamic history.

Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} reminded them of the letters he had received from
the people of Kūfah inviting him to come, informed them of the treachery that
had befallen Muslim ibn ʿAqīl \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, and reminded them of his
standing within the Ummah as the grandson of the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}. When it became clear that the Kūfans were unwilling to honor
their pledges, al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} sought to avoid bloodshed and
proposed a peaceful resolution: that he be allowed to return to Makkah; that he
be allowed to travel directly to Yazīd in al-Shām and pledge allegiance to him
personally; that he be allowed to proceed to one of the frontier regions and
participate in Jihād in the path of Allāh.

These proposals were conveyed by ʿUmar ibn Saʿd to ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād and
initially appeared to offer a reasonable solution that would avoid bloodshed and
preserve the sanctity of the Prophet's \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} family. It is reported
that Ibn Ziyād was at first inclined towards accepting the arrangement. However,
a deputy of Yazīd, Shimr ibn Dhī al-Jawshan, incited ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād to
force al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} into submission rather than negotiate with
him. He stroked Ibn Ziyād's ego to the point that Ibn Ziyād explicitly ordered
ʿUmar ibn Saʿd to secure the allegiance of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} through
ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād or else fight him, refusing to listen to any voices of
reason.

At this stage, the situation became increasingly tense. ʿUmar ibn Saʿd, despite
commanding the Kūfan forces, appears to have been reluctant to initiate
hostilities. Historical reports indicate that he hoped to avoid bloodshed and
bring the matter to a peaceful conclusion. Seeing this hesitation, Ibn Ziyād
dispatched Shimr ibn Dhī al-Jawshan to Karbalāʾ with strict instructions. If
ʿUmar ibn Saʿd refused to carry out the order to fight, Shimr was to assume
command in his place.

Before proceeding further, it is worth pausing to reflect upon the background of
Shimr ibn Dhī al-Jawshan, for his role in this tragedy makes the events of
Karbalāʾ even more painful.

Shimr was not merely a distant enemy. He had known ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} personally and had previously fought under him during the
Battle of Ṣiffīn. He also shared a tribal connection with the household of ʿAlī
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. The mother of al-ʿAbbās ibn ʿAlī \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}, Umm
al-Banīn Fāṭimah bint Ḥizām al-Kilābīyah, belonged to Banū Kilāb, the same
tribe as Shimr. Because of this relationship, he regarded the sons of Umm
al-Banīn as his kinsmen through their maternal lineage. Some even called him the
tribal brother-in-law of ʿAlī \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}.

On the day of ʿĀshūrāʾ, Shimr attempted to exploit this connection. The
historians relate that he approached the camp and called out, “Where are the
sons of our sister?” referring to al-ʿAbbās, ʿAbdullāh, Jaʿfar, and ʿUthmān, the
sons of Umm al-Banīn \hon{\P_THM}{\BODY}. He offered them a guarantee of safety
if they would abandon al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. Al-ʿAbbās
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} rejected the offer immediately and rebuked Shimr, declaring
that they would never forsake the grandson of the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}. (_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_)

Returning to the events of Karbalāʾ, seeing the reluctance of ʿUmar ibn Saʿd to
commence the battle, Ibn Ziyād replaced him with Shimr ibn Dhī al-Jawshan.
Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} refused to submit to the authority of Ibn Ziyād in
Kūfah. He did not cry and beg for his life as many Shia sources portray him to
have done. It is an insult to claim that the grandson of the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, the son of ʿAlī \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, and the son of Fāṭimah
\hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} would beg for his life to be spared by a random group of
men. The forces surrounding him insisted that he surrender and place himself
under Ibn Ziyād's judgment. When he refused, pressure upon his camp
intensified. Eventually, access to the waters of the Euphrates became restricted,
causing severe hardship for al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, his family, and his
companions.

Despite the growing hostility, the battle had not yet begun. It is interesting
to note that during the times of prayer in this period, the two groups would
cease all fighting and unite behind al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} as their Imam
in prayer, as everyone present knew that there was no one more deserving of that
position than him.

On the afternoon of the 9th of Muḥarram, the Kūfan forces began moving towards
the camp of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} in preparation for battle. Seeing the
army advance, al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} sent word requesting that they delay
the fighting until the following day. He asked for one final night so that he
and his companions could devote themselves to prayer, recitation of the Quran,
remembrance of Allāh, and supplication. His request was granted. That night was
spent in worship as the small band of believers prepared themselves for what
they knew was likely to be their final night in this world. The camp resonated
with the recitation of the Quran, _dhikr_, and earnest supplication throughout
the darkness.

When dawn arrived on the 10th of Muḥarram, al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}
addressed the opposing forces one final time. He reminded them of who he was,
the grandson of the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, the son of Fāṭimah
\hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}, and one of the two leaders of the youth of Paradise. He
reminded them of the hundreds of letters they had sent inviting him to Kūfah and
called upon them to fear Allāh and refrain from shedding sacred blood. Yet his
words fell upon hearts that had already been overtaken by fear, tribal
loyalties, political pressure, and the temptations of worldly gain. Ibn Ziyād
had rejected all attempts at a peaceful resolution. It was not an army
confronting an army; rather, it was a small group of loyal family members and
supporters standing against a force numbering in the thousands.

Among the most remarkable events of that morning was the repentance of al-Ḥurr
ibn Yazīd al-Tamīmī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}. Al-Ḥurr had initially been tasked with
intercepting al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and preventing him from returning to
Madīnah or proceeding elsewhere. As the reality of the situation became clear
and he realized that the army intended to kill the grandson of the Messenger of
Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, he became overwhelmed with regret. He abandoned the
Kūfan ranks, rode towards al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, sought his forgiveness,
and joined his camp. Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} accepted him with kindness,
and al-Ḥurr was later martyred while fighting in his defense. (_Al-Bidāyah wa
al-Nihāyah_)

But things began to take a negative turn. Despite many of his family members
being martyred, the initial signs of victory appeared to be with the camp of
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. Among the most famous of the martyrs was
al-ʿAbbās ibn ʿAlī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, the son of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and the standard-bearer of al-Ḥusayn's camp. Renowned for
his courage, loyalty, and devotion, al-ʿAbbās remained at the side of his
brother until the very end. The historical reports mention his efforts to secure
water for the camp after access to the Euphrates had been restricted. He was
martyred while trying to fetch water from the river.

Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} himself fought valiantly, displaying unmatched
bravery, courage, and steadfastness. As the battle intensified, the Kūfan forces
increasingly relied upon volleys of arrows to weaken the small group opposing
them. One by one, the companions and family members of al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} were killed until only a handful remained. Yet despite
witnessing the deaths of those closest to him, al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}
remained steadfast, patient, courageous, and completely reliant upon Allāh. It
was a family, not an army, that was fighting an army.

Eventually the overwhelming numerical advantage of the Kūfan army took its toll.
In the final moments of the battle, on Friday, the 10th of Muḥarram, 61 AH,
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} was severely wounded and fell to the ground at the
age of fifty-eight. (_Al-Muʿjam al-Kabīr_)

The enemies surrounded him, and Zurʿah ibn Sharīk struck a blow upon his
shoulder. A man named Sinān ibn Abī ʿAmr then advanced and attacked al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} with a spear, bringing him to the ground. Upon the orders of
Shimr, Sinān dismounted from his horse and killed him. Some reports suggest that
Khawlī ibn Yazīd al-Aṣbaḥī was the one who severed al-Ḥusayn's blessed head from
his body. (_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_)

After the martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, the historians carefully
documented the injuries found upon his blessed body. Imam Ibn Kathīr
\hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} writes, “It was observed that his body bore thirty-three
wounds inflicted by spears and thirty-four wounds caused by swords and other
weapons.”

He further states that the total number of those martyred alongside al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} was seventy-two. Muḥammad ibn al-Ḥanafīyah
\hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} related that seventeen of those martyrs were from the
descendants of Fāṭimah \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}, the daughter of the Messenger of
Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}. The tragedy occurred on Friday, the 10th of Muḥarram,
61 AH (10 October 680 CE), when al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} was approximately
fifty-four years and six months old. (_Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah_)

Shaykh Ehsan Elahi Zaheer \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} and other scholars stated that if
we assess the various narrations concerning Karbalāʾ, it can be concluded that
the actual battle lasted no more than two to three hours. The Shia exaggerate
the matter and portray it as though it continued for many days.

The blessed head of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} was then brought and placed
before ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād, who began poking at his teeth with a stick. Hands
tremble, and pens break as we write this. This was among the most despicable
crimes and heinous murders ever committed. It baffles the mind to even imagine
how intoxicated with the Dunyā these men must have been that they dared to raise
their swords against the grandson of the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}.
How will they stand before Allāh and His Messenger \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} on the Day
of Judgment? May the curse of Allāh be upon all those responsible, and may they
receive the harshest of punishments in the Hereafter.

In summary, we can conclude that three main groups were responsible for the
martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}:

- those who invited al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} to Kūfah and then abandoned him;
- ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād and his followers who proceeded to kill the Ahl al-Bayt
  on their own accord without any direct orders from al-Shām; and
- Sinān ibn Abī ʿAmr, who delivered the fatal blow that martyred al-Ḥusayn
  \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}.

One of the noble martyrs of Karbalāʾ was ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī ibn Abī
Ṭālib \hon{\P_THM}{\BODY}, the eldest son of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. His
mother was Laylá bint Abī Murrah ibn ʿUrwah ibn Masʿūd al-Thaqafī
\hon{\M_HER}{\BODY}. Laylá's mother was Maymūnah bint Abī Sufyān, making her the
granddaughter of Abū Sufyān ibn Ḥarb and the niece of Muʿāwiyah ibn Abī Sufyān
\hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}. The marriage of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} to Laylá took
place after the reconciliation between his brother al-Ḥasan \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}
and Muʿāwiyah \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} in 41 AH. (_Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrá_) This marriage
demonstrates that despite the political disagreements that had existed between
certain members of Banū Hāshim and Banū Umayyah, familial ties, marriages, and
mutual respect continued to exist between the two noble families. Such realities
stand in stark contrast to the later sectarian narratives that portray the
relationship between the two households as one of perpetual hatred and
hostility.

May Allāh accept them all amongst the martyrs and raise their ranks in the
highest levels of Jannah.

ʿAmmār ibn Abī ʿAmmār narrated that he said to Ibn ʿAbbās, “I saw the Messenger
of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} in a dream at midday. He appeared dishevelled and
dusty, carrying a bottle filled with blood.” I asked, “May my father and mother
be sacrificed for you, O Messenger of Allāh! What is this?” He replied, “This is
the blood of al-Ḥusayn and his companions. I have been collecting it since
today.” ʿAmmār said, “We recorded that day and later found that al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} had indeed been killed on that very day.” (_Musnad Aḥmad_)

Umm Salamah \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} is reported to have heard the Jinn lamenting
the martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} after his death. Several reports
concerning this are recorded by Imam Aḥmad, Ibn Abī al-Dunyā, and Ibn ʿAsākir,
although their chains are not free from weakness. Nevertheless, the scholars
frequently mention them among the extraordinary events reported in connection
with the martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}.

Years later, Sinān ibn Anas, one of the principal perpetrators involved in the
killing of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, visited al-Ḥajjāj ibn Yūsuf. A meeting
of tyrants and killers. Imam al-Ṭabarānī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} narrated that Aslam
al-Manqarī said, “After I entered al-Ḥajjāj's quarters, Sinān ibn Anas entered.
He was an elderly dark-complexioned man with henna-dyed hair, a long nose, and
marks upon his face. He stood before al-Ḥajjāj, who looked at him and asked,
‘Are you the one who killed al-Ḥusayn?’ He replied, ‘Yes.’ Al-Ḥajjāj asked, ‘How
did you do it?’ He said, ‘I struck him with my spear and attacked him with my
sword.’ Thereupon al-Ḥajjāj said, ‘You will never be gathered with him in one
abode.’” (_Al-Muʿjam al-Kabīr_) This report is among the strongest narrations
identifying Sinān ibn Anas as one of those directly involved in the martyrdom of
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}.
