
# Processions, Self-Flagellation, and _Taʿziyah_

The word _taʿziyah_ originally means condolence or an expression of grief. In
Persia, it came to refer to dramatic depictions and reenactments of the
martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, while in India it referred to
mourning processions in which miniature mausoleums and shrines, intended to
represent Karbalāʾ, were carried through the streets and buried on the 10th of
Muḥarram. This practice was a gift from the Indian subcontinent to the Shia.

Another origin story, which appears to be more widely established, traces this
practice to the Mongol ruler Tīmūr, commonly known as Tīmūr Lang (Tamerlane). He
was known as “the Lame” due to a physical disability. Tīmūr used to visit the
shrine of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} annually during Muḥarram. However,
during his invasion of India in 800--801 AH (1398--1399 CE), which culminated in
the conquest of Delhi, he was unable to make this journey. It is said that he
therefore ordered a replica of the shrine to be constructed so that the rituals
associated with the site could still be performed. To accommodate him, soil was
reportedly brought from Karbalāʾ, and due to time constraints, the replica was
fashioned from bamboo and paper. Thereafter, rulers in Awadh and other regions
around Delhi began imitating the practice. Their subjects, both Muslim and
non-Muslim, followed suit, and over time these ceremonies grew into elaborate
public spectacles. It was from this that the practice known as _Taʿziyadārī_
came into existence.

The practice continued during the era of the Mughal rulers. Shaykh Maqṣūd
al-Ḥasan Fayḍī \hon{\H_HIM}{\BODY} explained that from the first Mughal emperor
Bābur to the last Mughal emperor Bahādur Shāh Ẓafar, there was only one emperor
who openly adopted Shiism: Nāṣir al-Dīn Muḥammad, better known as Humāyūn. He
embraced Shiism after seeking assistance from the Persian Empire in reclaiming
his kingdom. Following his restoration to power through Persian support, a
number of his family members married into Shia families. In Persia, people would
gather on the 10th of Muḥarram to mourn at the grave of al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. Since travelling to Persia annually was not practical for
many, they began constructing _taʿziyah_ replicas and symbolic shrines, mourning
around them in a manner similar to what they had witnessed in Persia.

As the years passed, more and more practices were added. The people became
heavily influenced by Hindu customs and began imitating them. The Hindu festival
of Dussehra, which commemorates the triumph of Rāma over the ten-headed demon
king Rāvaṇa, is celebrated over a period of ten days. Likewise, some Muslims
began conducting ten-day processions during Muḥarram. Just as dramatic battles
are reenacted in Hindu performances of Rāmlīlā, scenes from Karbalāʾ began to be
reenacted in these processions.

Even Shia writers from the Arab lands acknowledged that such practices were
unknown among them and had originated in the Indian subcontinent. Seeing the
large crowds and widespread popularity these events attracted, Shia communities
elsewhere gradually adopted them as well.

Participating in such processions is completely impermissible and a grave sin,
as these _taʿziyah_ structures are often venerated, revered, and called upon
besides Allāh. In addition to the grave sin of _shirk_, these practices represent
one of the clearest examples of imitating and propagating the customs of the
disbelievers.

Shāh ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} was asked whether it is permissible to
attend gatherings in which _Taʿziyah_ replicas are displayed out of respect for
the Ahl al-Bayt or as an expression of grief over the martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. He replied,

> It is not permissible to attend such gatherings, even with the intention of
> reverence or grief, because there is nothing present that deserves to be
> venerated. They are merely structures fashioned from wood by those who
> construct the _Taʿziyah_. They are not deserving of reverence; rather, they
> are deserving of being destroyed.

> As the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, “Whoever among you sees an
> evil, let him change it with his hand. If he is unable, then with his tongue.
> If he is unable, then with his heart, and that is the weakest of faith.”
> (_Fatāwá ʿAzīzī_)

Self-flagellation is another infamous practice associated with Muḥarram. Shaykh
Ibn Ḥusayn writes in detail concerning this subject. Among the points he
mentions are that self-flagellation is a devotional act in which a person
strikes himself with whips or other instruments that inflict pain as an
expression of remorse or repentance. Such practices are completely impermissible
in Islam.

Historical studies indicate that self-flagellation rituals were practiced among
several Greco-Roman and Egyptian cults, including the cult of Isis in Egypt and
the Dionysian cults of Greece. Women were flogged during the Roman festival of
Lupercalia in the belief that it promoted fertility. In Christianity,
self-punishment through self-flagellation, known as the mortification of the
flesh, emerged in fourth-century Europe and became widespread during the
medieval period. It was performed as a means of repenting from sins and
participating in the alleged suffering of Jesus. In a similar manner,
self-flagellation within Shiism is often presented as a means of sharing in the
suffering of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and imitating his pain. Hinduism
likewise contains a number of self-punishment rituals and festivals, including
Garuḍan Thookkam and Thaipusam.

Although not all Shia engage in the more extreme forms of self-harm, such as
_Taṭbīr_ or _Qamah Zanī_ (striking the head with blades, swords, or knives) or
_Zanjīr Zanī_ with bladed chains, a number of prominent Āyatullāhs have
sanctioned, encouraged, and defended such practices, even when performed on
children. Whether blood is drawn or not, striking oneself with chains and
engaging in ritual self-harm remains a pagan innovation completely foreign to
Islam. These practices were unknown to the early Muslims and were later spread
by the Ṣafavids throughout Persia, Iraq, and the Indian subcontinent.

The Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, “He is not one of us who slaps
his cheeks, tears his clothes, and calls out with the calls of Jāhilīyah.”
(_Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī_)

In the Indian subcontinent, such practices are commonly referred to as _Nawḥah_ or
_Mātam_.

Even al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} himself, shortly before his martyrdom,
advised his beloved sister Zaynab \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} not to mourn for him in
such a manner. Imam al-Ṭabarī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} records that al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} said to his sister:

> My sister, fear Allāh and seek consolation through His decree. Know that the
> people of the earth die, and the inhabitants of the heavens do not remain
> forever. Everything will perish except the Face of Allāh. My father was better
> than me, my mother was better than me, and my brother was better than me. In
> the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} there is an example for me, for
> them, and for every Muslim.

He then consoled her and said, “My sister, I place you under oath: when I am
killed, do not tear your garment over me, do not strike your face, and do not
cry out with cries of destruction and ruin.” (_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_)

This does not mean that one should not feel sadness at the martyrdom of
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} or not grieve over the immense tragedy of
Karbalāʾ. Every believer who loves the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} and
his family feels sorrow when reflecting upon what occurred. The murder of the
beloved grandson of the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} was among the greatest
calamities to ever befall this Ummah. However, grief is one thing, and
innovation is another. Sadness is natural, but turning grief into annual rituals
of wailing, self-harm, chest-beating, tearing garments, and public displays of
lamentation is something entirely different.

The Ahl al-Bayt themselves experienced the pain of Karbalāʾ more deeply than
anyone else. They buried their fathers, brothers, sons, nephews, and relatives.
Yet despite their grief, they did not establish annual mourning ceremonies,
processions, self-flagellation, or the practices that later became associated
with Muḥarram.

Imam Abū Nuʿaym \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} records that ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn Zayn
al-ʿĀbidīn \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} was asked about his frequent weeping, whereupon
he replied, “Do not blame me. Yaʿqūb lost one son and wept until his eyes turned
white, even though he did not know whether he had died. As for me, I witnessed
fourteen men from my family being killed in a single day. Do you think their
grief will ever depart from my heart?” (_Ḥilyat al-Awliyāʾ_)

This statement clearly demonstrates the profound grief carried by the family of
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn wept, remembered, and mourned his
loved ones. Yet despite this immense sorrow, there is no report that he struck
himself, tore his garments, cursed the Companions, organized annual
commemorations, or encouraged others to engage in the practices that later
emerged amongst some groups. Rather, his grief remained within the limits
prescribed by the Sharīʿah.

The difference between the two approaches is clear. One is the grief of Yaʿqūb
\hon{\S_HIM}{\BODY}, the grief of Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, and the
grief of the righteous believers---patient, dignified, and restrained by
obedience to Allāh. The other is grief transformed into ritual, innovation, and
actions expressly prohibited by the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} and
rejected by al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} himself on the eve of his martyrdom.
True love for al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} is not demonstrated by opposing his
instructions. Rather, it is demonstrated by following his example, honoring his
sacrifice, loving the Ahl al-Bayt, hating the oppression that was committed
against them, and remaining firmly upon the guidance of the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} until the Day of Judgment.
