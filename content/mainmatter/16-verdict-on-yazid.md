
# The Scholarly Verdict on Yazīd

Following the death of Muʿāwiyah \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} in 60 AH, his son Yazīd
assumed leadership over the Muslim lands in accordance with the decision made by
his father. It is possible that Muʿāwiyah \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} made this decision
in an attempt to prevent further strife, division, and disunity within the
Ummah.

Al-Ḥāfiẓ ʿAbd al-Ghanī al-Maqdisī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} said regarding Yazīd, “His
Khilāfah was valid. Sixty of the Ṣaḥābah of the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, including Ibn ʿUmar, pledged allegiance to him.” This point
is mentioned merely to clarify a legal and historical matter. The validity of a
ruler's authority does not automatically mean that he was righteous, just, or
worthy of praise. Indeed, Yazīd's reign lasted little more than three years, yet
within that brief period occurred some of the greatest tragedies ever to befall
the Muslim Ummah: the martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} at
Karbalāʾ, the massacre of al-Ḥarrah in Madīnah, and the siege of Makkah during
which the Kaʿbah itself was struck by catapults. It is therefore unsurprising
that the scholars of Ahl al-Sunnah spoke extensively regarding him and differed
sharply concerning his status.

## Scholars Who Permitted Cursing Yazīd

Shāh Walī Allāh al-Dihlawī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} while explaining the _ḥadīth_,
“Then callers to misguidance will arise,” wrote, “The caller to misguidance in
al-Shām was Yazīd, and the caller to misguidance in Iraq was al-Mukhtār.”
(_Ḥujjat Allāh al-Bālighah_)

Imam al-Khallāl \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} narrated that Ṣāliḥ ibn Aḥmad
\hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} said, “I said to my father, ‘Some people claim that they
love Yazīd.’ He replied, ‘O my son, does anyone who believes in Allāh and the
Last Day love Yazīd?’ I said, ‘Then why do you not curse him?’ He replied, ‘O my
son, when have you ever seen your father curse anyone?’”

Imam al-Khallāl \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} also narrated that ʿAbd al-Malik al-Maymūnī
said, “I said to Aḥmad, ‘Was Yazīd not a drinker of wine?’ He replied, ‘That is
established with us.’ It was said to him, ‘Do you curse him?’ He replied, ‘How
can I not curse the one who did what he did?’” (_Kitāb al-Sunnah_)

This demonstrates that Imam Aḥmad \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} detested Yazīd, forbade
loving him, and considered cursing him permissible in principle, while
personally refraining from cursing specific individuals as a matter of personal
piety.

Al-Ḥāfiẓ Ibn Rajab al-Ḥanbalī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} did not support attributing
the cursing of Yazīd specifically to Imam Aḥmad. He wrote, “The statement of
Imam Aḥmad only establishes cursing the oppressors in general. There is no
explicit specification in it proving the permissibility of cursing Yazīd in
particular.” (_Dhayl Ṭabaqāt al-Ḥanābilah_) However, this argument does not
carry significant weight when the entirety of the evidence transmitted from Imam
Aḥmad is considered. Imam Ibn Rajab's statement addresses only one particular
narration and argues that it does not explicitly mention Yazīd by name. Yet
there are numerous other reports from Imam Aḥmad that go far beyond merely
cursing oppressors in general. Among them is the well-known narration of Ṣāliḥ
ibn Aḥmad \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} cited above.

Qāḍī Abū Yaʿlá al-Ḥanbalī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} said, “Whoever refrains from
cursing Yazīd is either unaware of the reality of the matter or is showing undue
leniency towards people. Some argue that a believer does not curse, but this
applies to one who does not deserve to be cursed. As for one who has become
deserving of curse through his actions, then what prevents his being cursed?”
(_Juzʾ fī Laʿn Yazīd_)

Imam Ibn Mufliḥ al-Ḥanbalī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} wrote, “Ṣāliḥ said, ‘I asked my
father, “They say that Allāh did not curse Yazīd.” He replied, “Rather, Allāh
cursed him in His Book.” I asked, “Where?” He recited,

\AYAH_AR_START
فَهَلۡ عَسَيۡتُمۡ إِن تَوَلَّيۡتُمۡ أَن تُفۡسِدُواْ فِي ٱلۡأَرۡضِ وَتُقَطِّعُوٓاْ أَرۡحَامَكُمۡ ٢٢ أُوْلَٰٓئِكَ ٱلَّذِينَ لَعَنَهُمُ ٱللَّهُ
\AYAH_AR_END

\AYAH_START
Would you then, if you were given authority, spread corruption in the land and
sever your ties of kinship? Those are the ones whom Allāh has cursed...
(Muḥammad, Q47:22--3)
\AYAH_END

Then he said, “And what severing of kinship is greater than the killing of
al-Ḥusayn?”’”

<!-- TODO: Spelling of Sharʿīyah -->

Ibn Mufliḥ then commented, “In this narration is proof that no believer in Allāh
and the Last Day has neither loyalty towards Yazīd nor defends him. It also
contains proof for the permissibility of cursing Yazīd. However, the preferred
view according to Aḥmad is silence regarding him due to the lack of any benefit
in cursing him, and this is what led some people to mistakenly think that Imam
Aḥmad prohibited cursing him.” (_Al-Ādāb al-Sharʿīyah_)

Imam Abū al-Faraj Ibn al-Jawzī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} said, “A man asked me about
Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiyah. I replied that he should not occupy himself with that
matter. He then asked me whether it is permissible to curse him. I answered that
the cautious and God-fearing scholars permitted cursing him, among them Imam
Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal. In fact, statements have been transmitted from Aḥmad regarding
Yazīd that are more severe than merely cursing him.” (_Al-Radd ʿalá
al-Mutaʿaṣṣib_) In another place, Imam Ibn al-Jawzī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} said,
“The pious scholars permitted cursing him, among them Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal.”
(_Al-Muntaẓam_)

Imam Ibn al-Jawzī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} further wrote, “The opponent says, ‘Why do
you not remain silent regarding Yazīd out of respect for his father Muʿāwiyah
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}?’ We reply, ‘Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal did not remain silent. Nor did
al-Khallāl, nor his student Abū Bakr ibn ʿAbd al-ʿAzīz, nor al-Qāḍī Abū Yaʿlá,
nor his son Abū al-Ḥusayn.’” (_Al-Radd ʿalá al-Mutaʿaṣṣib_)

It is also noteworthy that Imam Ibn al-Jawzī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} did not write
_al-Radd ʿalá al-Mutaʿaṣṣib al-ʿAnīd al-Māniʿ min Dhamm Yazīd_ in a vacuum.
Rather, it was composed as a direct response to one of the leading Ḥanbalī
scholars of his time, Abū Muḥammad ʿAbd al-Mughīth ibn Zuhayr al-Baghdādī
\hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} (d. 583 AH), who had authored a now-lost work entitled
_Faḍāʾil Yazīd_, arguing against criticizing or cursing Yazīd. Ibn al-Jawzī
relates that the dispute began after he was asked during one of his sermons
whether cursing Yazīd was permissible. Although he initially preferred silence,
when pressed he replied that “the pious scholars, including Imam Aḥmad,
permitted it.” When this reached ʿAbd al-Mughīth, he wrote in defence of Yazīd,
prompting Ibn al-Jawzī to compose his famous rebuttal.

In _Ṭabaqāt al-Ḥanābilah_, it is mentioned regarding Ibn Abī Yaʿlá
\hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, “He authored a work concerning those whom it is permissible
to curse, and he included Yazīd among them. He said, ‘The one who refrains from
cursing him is either ignorant of the permissibility of doing so or is a
hypocrite seeking to deceive the people into believing that it is
impermissible.’”

Imam al-Saffārīnī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} further said, “Most of the later scholars
among the _ḥuffāẓ_ and _mutakallimīn_ permitted cursing Yazīd---may Allāh curse
him. How could they not, when he is the one who committed vile atrocities,
violated the sacred limits, tore away veils, permitted Madīnah to be plundered,
killed al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, his family, his children, and the Ahl
al-Bayt. He was notorious for drinking alcohol, immorality, and wickedness. Some
of the _ḥuffāẓ_ have even transmitted poetry attributed to him which clearly
indicates disbelief. I have no doubt that whoever uttered such words has exited
the fold of Islam.” (_Ghidhāʾ al-Albāb_)

In another place, Imam al-Saffārīnī al-Ḥanbalī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} said,
“Yazīd's approval of the killing of al-Ḥusayn and his rejoicing over it has been
stated by more than one Imam to be _mutawātir_ in meaning.” He further said,
“More than one Imam has explicitly stated that Yazīd's pleasure at the killing
of al-Ḥusayn and his rejoicing over it are established through recurrent reports
in meaning, and some scholars considered this sufficient grounds for declaring
disbelief.” (_Lawāmiʿ al-Anwār al-Bahiyyah_)

Imam al-Taftāzānī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} adopted one of the strongest positions and
wrote, “The truth is that Yazīd's approval of the killing of al-Ḥusayn, his
delight in it, and his mistreatment of the household of the Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} are matters whose meaning is established through numerous
reports. Therefore, we do not hesitate regarding his affair. May the curse of
Allāh be upon him, his supporters, and his helpers.” (_Sharḥ al-ʿAqāʾid
al-Nasafiyyah_)

## Was Yazīd a Drinker of Wine?

The issue of Yazīd's personal conduct was also discussed extensively by the
scholars.

As mentioned previously, Imam Aḥmad \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} stated, “That is
established with us.”

Imam Ibn Kathīr \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} recorded reports stating, “Yazīd committed
repugnant acts. There was oppression and aggression in him, and he consumed
intoxicants.” (_Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah_)

<!-- TODO: What is a Nāṣibī? -->

Al-Ḥāfiẓ al-Dhahabī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} said, “Yazīd was a Nāṣibī, harsh,
coarse, and crude. He consumed intoxicants and committed reprehensible acts. His
reign began with the killing of the martyr al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and
ended with the tragedy of al-Ḥarrah. Consequently, the people came to hate him,
Allāh did not place blessing in his life, and many rose in rebellion against him
after the martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn.” (_Siyar Aʿlām al-Nubalāʾ_)

Al-ʿAllāmah Ibn al-Athīr \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} narrated from al-Mundhir ibn
al-Zubayr, “By Allāh, Yazīd used to drink intoxicants. By Allāh, I visited him
while he was intoxicated.” In another narration he said, “Yazīd granted me one
hundred thousand dirhams, but that will not prevent me from speaking the truth
about him.” (_Al-Kāmil fī al-Tārīkh_)

<!-- TODO: Check transliteration of al-Kiyā -->

Among those who openly permitted cursing Yazīd was the distinguished Shāfiʿī
jurist, theologian, _muḥaddith_, and Chief Judge of the Seljuk Empire, Abū
al-Ḥasan ʿAlī al-Ṭabarī, better known as al-Kiyā al-Harrāsī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}
(d. 504 AH). He was one of the foremost students of Imam al-Ḥaramayn al-Juwaynī
\hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} and served as one of the leading professors of the
Niẓāmīyah Madrasah in Baghdad, the most prestigious institution of Sunni
learning in his era.

Imam Ibn Khallikān \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} records that al-Kiyā al-Harrāsī was asked
about Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiyah, whereupon he replied,

> Yazīd is not counted amongst the Companions, for he was born during the
> Khilāfah of ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. As for the position of
> our early scholars regarding him, Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal had two narrations: one
> permitting only indirect censure and another permitting his explicit cursing.
> Mālik and Abū Ḥanīfah likewise have two reported positions concerning him. As
> for me, I hold only one position: that Yazīd should be openly cursed.

> How could I say otherwise when it is well known that he played backgammon,
> hunted with trained leopards, and was addicted to intoxicants? His poetry
> regarding wine is famous. Among what is attributed to him is: “When the
> wine-cup gathered my companions, and the singers encouraged the delights of
> love, I told them to enjoy themselves fully and take their pleasure, for even
> the longest-lasting things eventually come to an end.”

Imam Ibn Khallikān further relates that al-Kiyā al-Harrāsī wrote a lengthy
response on the matter and then added on the reverse side of the page, “Had
there been more space available to me, I would have written an even longer
answer exposing the disgraceful actions and scandals of this man.” (_Wafayāt
al-Aʿyān_)

## Al-Ḥarrah and the Siege of Makkah

Among the greatest crimes associated with Yazīd's reign was the tragedy of
al-Ḥarrah.

Imam Aḥmad ibn Ḥanbal \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} narrated in his _Musnad_ from al-Sāʾib
ibn Khallād \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} that the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}
said, “Whoever frightens the people of Madīnah unjustly, upon him is the curse
of Allāh, the angels, and all of mankind. Allāh will not accept from him any
obligatory or voluntary deed on the Day of Resurrection.”

Imam al-Bukhārī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} recorded on the authority of Abū Hurayrah
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} that the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said,
“Whoever intends harm to the people of this city (Madīnah), Allāh will melt him
as salt melts in water.” (_Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī_)

<!-- TODO: Footnote for Ḥanẓalah al-Ghasīl? -->

The incident of al-Ḥarrah was preceded by growing resentment amongst the people
of Madīnah towards Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiyah. After the martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and the increasing reports concerning Yazīd's conduct, a
delegation from Madīnah travelled to Damascus and met him personally. Upon
returning, several members of the delegation openly criticized him, describing
behavior they considered unbecoming of a Muslim ruler. This intensified
opposition to Yazīd within Madīnah, leading many of its inhabitants, including
prominent figures from the Anṣār and Quraysh, to renounce their allegiance to
him under the leadership of ʿAbdullāh ibn Ḥanẓalah \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, the son
of Ḥanẓalah al-Ghasīl.

In response, Yazīd dispatched a large Syrian army under the command of Muslim
ibn ʿUqbah al-Murrī to suppress the revolt. The two sides met at al-Ḥarrah, a
volcanic plain on the outskirts of Madīnah, in Dhū al-Ḥijjah of 63 AH. The
people of Madīnah were defeated, after which the city was subjected to one of
the darkest tragedies in Islamic history.

<!-- TODO: The following two paragraphs look very similar -->

Imam Ibn Ḥajar al-ʿAsqalānī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} wrote, “When the people of
Madīnah renounced allegiance to Yazīd in 63 AH, he dispatched Muslim ibn ʿUqbah
al-Murrī with a large army. He fought them, defeated them, and killed many of
the Companions, their sons, the leading Tābiʿīn, and the people of the city. He
then permitted Madīnah to be plundered for three days, during which terrible
atrocities occurred.” (_Fatḥ al-Bārī_)

Likewise, Imam Ibn Kathīr \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} wrote, “When the people of Madīnah
renounced allegiance to Yazīd, he dispatched Muslim ibn ʿUqbah with a large
army. He fought them, defeated them, killed many of the Companions, their sons,
and the leading Tābiʿīn, and then permitted Madīnah to be violated for three
days.” (_Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah_)

Imam Ibn ʿAsākir \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} records, “Al-Ḥasan mentioned the Day of
al-Ḥarrah and said, ‘By Allāh, scarcely anyone escaped from them.’ He then
related that the two sons of Zaynab bint Umm Salamah \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}, the
stepdaughter of the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, were killed. When
their bodies were brought before her, she said, ‘By Allāh, my grief over both of
you is immense. Yet my grief for this one is greater than for the other,’ and
she pointed to one of them, ‘For this one drew his sword, and I do not know what
actions he committed. As for the other, he remained inside his home, yet they
entered his home and killed him there. It is for him that I have greater hope.’”
(_Tārīkh Dimashq_)

Imam al-Khallāl \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} narrates from Muḥammad ibn ʿAlī, who said,

> Muthannā related that he asked Imam Aḥmad about Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiyah. Imam
> Aḥmad replied, “Is he not the one who did what he did in Madīnah?” I asked,
> “And what did he do?” He said, “He killed the people of Madīnah from among the
> Ṣaḥābah of the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} and committed other atrocities.” I
> asked, “What else did he do?” He replied, “He permitted it to be plundered.”

> I then asked, “May _ḥadīth_ be narrated from him?” Imam Aḥmad replied, “No
> _ḥadīth_ should be narrated from him, nor should anyone record _ḥadīth_ from
> him.” I further asked, “Who was with him in Madīnah when these events took
> place?” He replied, “The people of al-Shām.” I asked, “And the people of
> Egypt?” He said, “No. The people of Egypt were only with them in the affair of
> ʿUthmān.” (_Kitāb al-Sunnah_)

After crushing the revolt in Madīnah, Muslim ibn ʿUqbah proceeded towards
Makkah, in accordance with Yazīd's instructions, to confront ʿAbdullāh ibn
al-Zubayr \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}, who had emerged as Yazīd's principal rival in the
Ḥijāz. However, Muslim died on the journey and transferred command to al-Ḥuṣayn
ibn Numayr al-Sakūnī. The Syrian army then continued towards Makkah and laid
siege to Ibn al-Zubayr.

During the course of the siege, catapults were erected around the city, and
stones were launched towards Makkah. As a result, parts of the Kaʿbah were
damaged, and a fire broke out which affected the Sacred House.

Imam Ibn Kathīr \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} writes, “Then al-Ḥuṣayn ibn Numayr marched
to Makkah and besieged Ibn al-Zubayr. He erected catapults against the Kaʿbah
until part of its eastern wall was destroyed, and a great fire broke out which
burned a large portion of the Kaʿbah.” (_Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah_)

Whilst the siege was still ongoing, news arrived that Yazīd had died in al-Shām
in Rabīʿ al-Awwal of 64 AH. Upon receiving the news, al-Ḥuṣayn ibn Numayr
entered into discussions with Ibn al-Zubayr and eventually withdrew with the
Syrian army, bringing the siege to an end.

A question is often raised: if Yazīd died before the siege concluded, why do so
many scholars continue to attribute the assault upon Makkah and the violation of
its sanctity to him? The answer is that the army was his army, the campaign was
his campaign, and the commanders involved were acting under his authority. The
scholars therefore assigned political responsibility to Yazīd even if he was not
physically present and even if certain actions were carried out by his governors
and military commanders. This is similar to how the scholars frequently
attribute the tragedy of Karbalāʾ to Yazīd despite the immediate perpetrators
being ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād, Shimr ibn Dhī al-Jawshan, Sinān ibn Anas, and
others. The reason is that all of these events occurred under Yazīd's rule and
through officials acting on his behalf.

Imam Ibn Ḥazm al-Ẓāhirī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} said,

> This criminal (Yazīd) dishonored Islam openly and shamelessly. He permitted
> Madīnah to be violated for three days, during which the Ṣaḥābah of the
> Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} were subjected to oppression, assault,
> and the looting of their homes.

> Thereafter, that army proceeded to Makkah---may Allāh honor it---and besieged
> it, pelting it with stones launched from catapults. Their commander was
> al-Ḥuṣayn ibn Numayr al-Sakūnī, who assumed command after Mujrim ibn ʿUqbah
> al-Murrī. He was originally named Muslim, but we call him Mujrim (criminal).
> Three nights after the event of al-Ḥarrah, he died. Then Allāh Almighty seized
> Yazīd with a mighty seizure and put an end to his life. Yazīd died on the
> fifteenth of Rabīʿ al-Awwal in the year 64 AH. He was just over thirty years
> old. His mother was Maysūn bint Baḥdal al-Kalbīyah. His reign lasted three
> years and eight months.” (Rasāʾil Ibn Ḥazm)

The enormity of these events led many later scholars to regard Karbalāʾ,
al-Ḥarrah, and the siege of Makkah as sufficient grounds for severe condemnation
of Yazīd, regardless of the disagreements concerning his personal culpability in
every individual detail.

Imam al-Suyūṭī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} addressed those who seek to defend Yazīd on
the basis of his participation in the campaign against Constantinople, “No
virtue from Jihād is established for Yazīd except that he accompanied his father
to Constantinople under compulsion, for he was preoccupied with hunting and
drinking.” He further wrote, “During his reign occurred the massacre of
al-Ḥarrah, in which many Companions and their sons were killed, and Madīnah was
violated for three days. Thereafter the Kaʿbah was bombarded with catapults
until it caught fire.” (_Tārīkh al-Khulafāʾ_)

In a succinct summary of the calamities associated with Yazīd's reign, Shaykh
al-Ḥanābilah Marʿī al-Karmī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} said,

> After Muʿāwiyah, his son Yazīd assumed authority. He was a man of evil conduct
> and was known for vile actions. The greatest of these was the killing of
> al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, the captivity of the family of the Messenger of
> Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, and the dispatch of an army against Madīnah which
> committed infamous acts of killing and corruption.

> It is said that three hundred virgins were violated, approximately three
> hundred Ṣaḥābah were killed, and seven hundred reciters of the Quran were
> slain. The Friday and congregational prayers ceased for days in the Prophet's
> Mosque until dogs entered it and urinated on the _minbar_.

> Thereafter he sent an army against Ibn al-Zubayr in Makkah, which bombarded
> the Kaʿbah with catapults and set it ablaze. Nothing more disgraceful occurred
> than what took place during his reign and because of him. (_Aqāwīl al-Thiqāt
> fī Taʾwīl al-Asmāʾ wa al-Ṣifāt_)

Imam Ibn al-ʿImād al-Ḥanbalī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} wrote,

> The scholars are agreed regarding the correctness of ʿAlī's fighting against
> his opponents because he was the rightful Imam. They likewise transmitted
> consensus regarding praising the uprising of al-Ḥusayn against Yazīd, the
> uprising of Ibn al-Zubayr and the people of the Two Sanctuaries against the
> Umayyads, and the uprising of Ibn al-Ashʿath and those with him from amongst the
> senior Tābiʿīn and the best of the Muslims against al-Ḥajjāj. Thereafter, the
> majority of the scholars held that it is permissible to rise against rulers such
> as Yazīd and al-Ḥajjāj. Some even permitted rising against every oppressive
> ruler.

> Ibn Ḥazm counted the four great wounds of Islam as: the killing of ʿUthmān,
> the killing of al-Ḥusayn, the tragedy of al-Ḥarrah, and the killing of Ibn
> al-Zubayr. (Shadharāt al-Dhahab)

The martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, the slaughter of the people of
Madīnah at al-Ḥarrah, and the assault upon Makkah remain among the darkest
chapters in Islamic history. The grandson of the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} was killed, the city of the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} was violated, and the Sacred House of Allāh was struck by
catapults, all within the span of a few years.

The believer does not read these events with indifference. His heart grieves for
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, for the family of the Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, for the people of Madīnah, and for every innocent soul that
was wronged.

## Scholars Who Opposed Cursing Yazīd

Few periods in Islamic history witnessed tragedies as devastating as those that
occurred during the reign of Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiyah. The martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, the massacre of al-Ḥarrah, and the siege of Makkah left
wounds that would be remembered by the Ummah for centuries. For this reason,
many of the leading scholars of Islam spoke about Yazīd in the harshest of
terms, with some even permitting or recommending that he be cursed by name.

At the same time, the issue was not one on which all scholars agreed. A number
of prominent Imams, despite condemning the atrocities that occurred during his
reign, opposed cursing Yazīd specifically and preferred either silence regarding
him or entrusting his affair to Allāh. Academic honesty therefore requires that
their position also be presented before a conclusion is reached.

Among the scholars who opposed cursing Yazīd by name was Ḥujjat al-Islām Abū
Ḥāmid al-Ghazzālī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}. Imam al-Ghazzālī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}
stated, “It has not been established that he killed al-Ḥusayn, nor that he
ordered his killing, nor that he approved of it. When such matters are not
established, it is not permissible to harbor negative assumptions concerning a
Muslim, for Allāh Most High says, ‘O you who believe, avoid much suspicion;
indeed some suspicion is sin.’ Therefore, it is obligatory to maintain a good
opinion regarding a Muslim whenever possible.” (_Wafayāt al-Aʿyān_)

In another work, he said, “And if it is asked, ‘Is it permissible to curse Yazīd
because of his murder of al-Ḥusayn or ordering it?’ We respond, ‘This fact is
unverified and, as such, it is impermissible to even assert that Yazīd killed
al-Ḥusayn or ordered his murder since this is an unverified accusation. Thus
cursing is certainly impermissible since it is not permitted to accuse a Muslim
of a major sin (such as murder) without evidence. Verily, it is permitted to
state that Ibn Muljam killed ʿAlī and Abū Luʾluʾ killed ʿUmar because that has
been established by clear proofs and is narrated by multiple chains. It is
absolutely impermissible to accuse another Muslim of sinfulness or disbelief
without clear evidence.”

He continued and said, “...if it is asked, ‘Is it permissible to say, “May Allāh
curse the murderer of al-Ḥusayn,” or, “May Allāh curse the one who ordered the
murder of al-Ḥusayn”?’, we say that it is correct to say, ‘May Allāh curse the
murderer of al-Ḥusayn if he died before repenting,’ because there exists the
possibility that the individual in question may have repented of his sinful
action before his death. Indeed, Wahshī, the murderer of Ḥamzah (ibn ʿAbd
al-Muṭṭalib) the uncle of the Prophet, murdered Ḥamzah when he was still a
disbeliever, but later repented from disbelief and his murderous act and thus it
is not permissible to curse him. Even though murder constitutes a major sin, it
is not disbelief, so even if it was unproven that he sincerely repented from his
act of murder, there would still be a major danger in cursing him. However,
there is no danger in remaining silent, and this should be the preferred course
of action.” (_Iḥyāʾ ʿUlūm al-Dīn_)

Imam Taqī al-Dīn Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} was among those scholars who
opposed both declaring Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiyah a disbeliever and cursing him by
name. Ibn Ḥajar al-Haytamī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} relates, “I saw in the Fatāwá of
Ibn al-Ṣalāḥ that he was asked about a person who cursed Yazīd solely on the
basis that he ordered the killing of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. He replied
that the claim that Yazīd ordered the killing of al-Ḥusayn is not established
according to us. Therefore, cursing Yazīd is not permissible, for abusing and
cursing a Muslim is not the characteristic of a believer.” (_Al-Ṣawāʿiq
al-Muḥriqah_)

Qāḍī Abū Bakr Ibn al-ʿArabī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} likewise opposed declaring Yazīd
a disbeliever or cursing him by name. He wrote, “If it is said that justice and
knowledge are among the conditions of the Khilāfah, and that Yazīd possessed
neither justice nor knowledge, then we ask, ‘What evidence establishes that
Yazīd possessed neither justice nor knowledge?’” (_Al-ʿAwāṣim min al-Qawāṣim_)

He also remarked, “Where are those historians who wrote against Yazīd, accusing
him of drinking wine and openly committing sins? Do they not possess any sense
of shame?” (_Al-ʿAwāṣim min al-Qawāṣim_) Many Mālikī scholars themselves refuted
Imam Ibn al-ʿArabī's treatment of Yazīd.

Although these scholars opposed cursing Yazīd by name, they did not deny the
enormity of Karbalāʾ, al-Ḥarrah, or the siege of Makkah. Rather, their objection
centred upon whether it had been conclusively established that Yazīd personally
ordered or approved these crimes.

## Shaykh al-Islām Ibn Taymīyah's Reconciliation

Shaykh al-Islām Ibn Taymīyah \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} tried to navigate the
differences that some scholars might have had on the topic and said,

<!-- TODO: Add footnote to the following -->

> People have differed concerning Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiyah into three groups: two
> extremes and one moderate group.

> One group exaggerated regarding him and claimed that he was a disbeliever, a
> hypocrite, and that he deliberately sought the killing of al-Ḥusayn in order
> to take revenge upon the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} for what his
> forefathers suffered at Badr and elsewhere. Such statements and similar claims
> are made by those Rāfiḍah who declare Abū Bakr, ʿUmar, and ʿUthmān to be
> disbelievers, so declaring Yazīd a disbeliever is even easier for them.

> The second group exaggerated in the opposite direction. They claimed that he was
> a righteous and just man, that he was among the Companions, and that he was one
> of the righteous friends of Allāh. Some even claimed that he was a Prophet. This
> view is also clearly false.

> The third position is that he was one of the kings of the Muslims. He
> possessed good deeds and bad deeds. He was born during the caliphate of
> ʿUthmān and was not a disbeliever. However, because of him the killing of
> al-Ḥusayn took place, and because of him what occurred at al-Ḥarrah occurred.
> He was neither a Companion nor one of the righteous friends of Allāh. This is
> the view of the majority of the people of reason, knowledge, and Ahl al-Sunnah
> wa al-Jamāʿah.

> Then those who adopted this position divided into three groups: one group
> cursed him, one group loved him, and one group neither cursed him nor loved
> him. This is what has been reported from Imām Aḥmad and others from the
> fair-minded scholars among the Muslims.

Ṣāliḥ ibn Aḥmad said, “I said to my father, ‘Some people claim that they love
Yazīd.’ He replied, ‘My son, would anyone who believes in Allāh and the Last Day
love Yazīd?’ I said, ‘Then why do you not curse him?’ He replied, ‘My son, when
have you ever known your father to curse anyone?’”

Abū Muḥammad al-Maqdisī said, “When he was asked about Yazīd, he said,
‘According to what I have heard, he is neither to be loved nor cursed.’ He also
said, ‘I heard our grandfather Abū ʿAbdillāh Ibn Taymīyah being asked about
Yazīd, and he replied, “We do not deny whatever good qualities he may have
possessed, nor do we exaggerate regarding him.”’” Then he said, “This is the most
just of the positions.” (_Majmūʿ al-Fatāwá_)



During a conversation with Bulāy, the Tatar ruler, in Damascus, Shaykh al-Islām
Ibn Taymīyah \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} declared, “May the curse of Allāh, His angels,
and all mankind be upon those who shed the blood of al-Ḥusayn, assisted in his
killing, or approved of it. May Allāh neither lessen their punishment nor grant
them respite.” (_Majmūʿ al-Fatāwá_)

Shaykh al-Islām Ibn Taymīyah \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} further wrote,

> Allāh honored al-Ḥusayn with martyrdom and disgraced those who killed him,
> assisted in his killing, or approved of it. Al-Ḥusayn was a continuation of
> the path of the earlier martyrs of Islam. He and his brother are the leaders
> of the youth of Jannah. Since they grew up after the major struggles endured
> by the earliest Muslims, Allāh honored them with martyrdom, thereby raising
> their rank and increasing their virtue.

> His martyrdom was indeed one of the greatest calamities. However, Allāh has
> instructed us, when afflicted by calamity, to say, “Indeed we belong to Allāh,
> and indeed to Him we shall return.” He also says, “Give glad tidings to the
> patient---those who, when calamity strikes them, say, ‘Indeed we belong to
> Allāh and indeed to Him we shall return. Upon such people are blessings and
> mercy from their Lord, and they are the rightly guided.’” (Q2:155--6; _Majmūʿ
> al-Fatāwá_)

In more recent times, Shaykh Muḥammad ibn Ṣāliḥ al-ʿUthaymīn \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}
summarized the discussion in a manner remarkably similar to that of Shaykh
al-Islām Ibn Taymīyah. He stated, “Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiyah was not from the
Ṣaḥābah. During his reign, great evils occurred, among them the killing of
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. The scholars of Ahl al-Sunnah differed regarding
him. Some cursed him, some declared him a sinner, and others remained silent
concerning him.

The correct view is that he was a sinful and oppressive ruler. He is neither to
be loved, nor praised, nor prayed for. However, if it is established that he
ordered the killing of al-Ḥusayn or was pleased with it, then he deserves to be
cursed because this is among the gravest of major sins. May the curse of Allāh
be upon whoever killed al-Ḥusayn, ordered his killing, or was pleased with it.”
(_Fatāwā Nūr ʿalá al-Darb_)

Similarly, among contemporary scholars, Muftī Muḥammad Shafīʿ ʿUthmānī
\hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, the father of Muftī Muḥammad Taqī ʿUthmānī
\hon{\H_HIM}{\BODY}, adopted a balanced position regarding Yazīd. While
acknowledging reports that Yazīd outwardly expressed remorse after the martyrdom
of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, he cautioned against viewing this as a genuine
exoneration of his conduct. He wrote,

> As for the prompt remorse shown by Yazīd and his outwardly honorable treatment
> of the remaining members of the Ahl al-Bayt, whether it was merely an attempt
> to erase the stain of his ill repute, or whether some fear of Allāh and
> concern for the Hereafter had genuinely arisen in him, this is known only to
> the All-Knowing and All-Aware.

> However, Yazīd's deeds and actions thereafter are likewise filled with acts of
> wickedness. Even as he lay dying, he dispatched armies for an assault upon
> al-Makkah al-Mukarramah, and he died in that very state. May Allāh deal with
> him according to what he deserves. (_Shahīd-i Karbalāʾ_)

Shaykh Aḥmad al-Sirhindī wrote, “Yazīd was deprived of true blessings and
belonged to the people of wickedness. As for cursing him, this remains a matter
over which there is hesitation based upon the principle of Ahl al-Sunnah of
refraining from cursing specific individuals. However, this does not mean that
Yazīd is free from deserving the consequences of curse.” (_Maktūbāt-i Imām
Rabbānī_)

The discussion concerning Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiyah remained a matter of disagreement
amongst the scholars of Ahl al-Sunnah. Some permitted cursing him, some
prohibited it, and others preferred silence. However, they were all united in
condemning the murder of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, the tragedy of
al-Ḥarrah, and the assault upon Makkah.

## The Name Yazīd and Historical Misconceptions

One of the ironies of later sectarian polemics is the hostility shown towards
the very name "Yazīd" simply because it was borne by Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiyah. In
reality, Yazīd was a common Arab name used by the Ṣaḥābah, their descendants,
and countless Muslims throughout Islamic history.

Among the Ṣaḥābah was Yazīd ibn Abī Sufyān \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}, the elder
brother of Muʿāwiyah \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. He was among the early Muslims,
participated in the conquest of al-Shām, and was appointed governor of parts of
Syria by Abū Bakr and ʿUmar \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} before passing away during the
plague of ʿAmwās. (_Al-Iṣābah_)

Likewise, Yazīd ibn al-Aswad al-ʿĀmirī \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} was a Ṣaḥābī of the
Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} who accepted Islam during his youth and
witnessed the Farewell Pilgrimage. Among his most famous narrations is that he
said,

<!-- TODO: Mina -->

> I performed the morning prayer with the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}
> at Masjid al-Khayf in Minā. After completing the prayer, he noticed two men at
> the back who had not joined the congregation. They were brought before him,
> trembling with fear. The Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} asked, “What prevented
> you from praying with us?” They replied, “O Messenger of Allāh, we had already
> prayed in our dwellings.” The Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} said, “Do not do
> that. If you have prayed in your dwellings and then come to a mosque in which
> the congregation is being established, then pray with them, for it will be
> counted as a voluntary prayer for you.”

This narration was recorded by Imam al-Tirmidhī, Imam al-Nasāʾī, Imam Abū Dāwūd,
and others, and is regarded as one of the principal evidences concerning
repeating a prayer with the congregation after having already prayed
individually. Yazīd ibn al-Aswad \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} later settled in al-Shām.

More remarkably, among those martyred alongside al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} at
Karbalāʾ were individuals who themselves bore the name Yazīd. The historians
mention, amongst them, Yazīd ibn Ziyād ibn al-Muhāṣir al-Kindī, Yazīd ibn
Thubayṭ al-ʿAbdī, and Yazīd ibn ʿAbdillāh ibn Thubayṭ \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}. Yazīd
ibn Thubayṭ al-ʿAbdī was a well-known supporter of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}
from Baṣrah, while Yazīd ibn Ziyād ibn al-Muhāṣir al-Kindī, better known as Abū
al-Shaʿthāʾ, was among the famous archers who fought and were martyred alongside
him. Their presence at Karbalāʾ demonstrates that the name itself carried no
negative connotation among the early Muslims.

Islam judges individuals by their deeds, not by their names, tribes, or family
connections. The sins of one man do not render blameworthy a name borne by
righteous Muslims, martyrs, scholars, and believers throughout the centuries.
Likewise, responsibility for wrongdoing is not inherited. The crimes of Yazīd
ibn Muʿāwiyah, whatever one's position regarding him, do not automatically
extend to his father, his children, his relatives, or anyone else who had no
part in those events.

This principle is especially important in the case of Muʿāwiyah ibn Abī Sufyān
\hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}. Muʿāwiyah was a Ṣaḥābī of the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, one of the scribes of revelation, and among those whom the
Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} supplicated for. He embraced Islam during the
lifetime of the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, accompanied him, narrated _aḥādīth_
from him, and later played a central role in the expansion and administration of
the Muslim state. Whatever differences later arose concerning political events
during his Khilāfah, the scholars of Ahl al-Sunnah have consistently
distinguished between Muʿāwiyah \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and his son Yazīd, judging
each according to his own actions and merits. As Allāh says,

\AYAH_AR_START
وَلَا تَزِرُ وَازِرَةٞ وِزۡرَ أُخۡرَىٰۚ
\AYAH_AR_END

\AYAH_START
No bearer of burdens shall bear the burden of another. (Al-Anʿām, Q6:164)
\AYAH_END

This is from the justice of Islam: every soul is judged according to its own
actions and stands before Allāh accountable only for what it has earned.
