
# The Aftermath of Karbalāʾ

After the battle, Yazīd's forces plundered the camps. Imam al-Ṭabarī
\hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} narrates from Ḥumayd ibn Muslim, “I reached the tent of ʿAlī
ibn al-Ḥusayn (Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn) \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} and found him ill, lying on
a bed. I heard Shimr ibn Dhī al-Jawshan saying to his men, ‘Why have we not yet
killed this one?’ I exclaimed, ‘_Subḥān Allāh_! Do you wish to kill a child?’ I
continued pushing them back until ʿUmar ibn Saʿd arrived. Seeing the situation,
he immediately ordered that none should enter the tent in which the women were
present and that the ill youngster be left alone. He also ordered that all the
looted possessions be returned, but nothing was brought back.”

At that moment, ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} thanked ʿUmar ibn Saʿd,
saying, “May you be rewarded, for indeed your words have saved us from great
danger.” (_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_) Thus, through the decree of Allāh, ʿAlī Zayn
al-ʿĀbidīn \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} survived the tragedy and would go on to become
the sole surviving adult male through whom the lineage of al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} continued.

It is also reported that after the martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY},
some of the attackers set fire to parts of the encampment. Ḥumayd ibn Muslim
relates that the women and children fled from tent to tent as the flames spread
amongst the camps. Some later historians mention that certain Kūfans feared
exposure because of the letters they had previously sent inviting al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} to Kūfah.

After this, the enemy forces took the surviving family members of al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, including his sister Zaynab and his son ʿAlī Zayn
al-ʿĀbidīn \hon{\M_TWO}{\BODY}, as captives to Kūfah.

When they entered the city, many of the people of Kūfah began weeping and
lamenting upon seeing the family of the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}
being led through the streets. The sight of the women and children of the
Prophet's household in captivity stirred emotions even amongst those who had
remained silent when al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} needed their support most.

It is reported that Zaynab \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY} addressed the people and rebuked
them for their hypocrisy and betrayal. She reminded them that they had invited
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, pledged their allegiance to him, and then
abandoned him at the moment of trial. Seeing the people crying before her, she
is reported to have said, “Do you weep? May your tears never cease and may your
wailing never end. You are like the woman who unravels her thread after
strengthening it. You have disgraced yourselves with a deed that can never be
washed away. How can you cleanse yourselves from the shame of killing the
grandson of the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}?”

Likewise, ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} addressed the
people and said, “I adjure you by Allāh, do you know that you wrote to my
father, deceived him, betrayed him, and then fought and killed him?” The people
replied, “Yes.” He then said, “May you perish for what your own souls have
prepared for yourselves.” (_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_; _Al-Bidāyah wa al-Nihāyah_)

Among the reports preserved in the Shia work _al-Iḥtijāj_ is the account of the
family of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} entering Kūfah after the tragedy of
Karbalāʾ. When the people saw the captives, they began weeping, crying aloud,
and striking their faces in remorse over what had taken place. Abū Manṣūr Aḥmad
ibn ʿAlī al-Ṭabarsī recorded that seeing the Kūfans, ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn Zayn
al-ʿĀbidīn \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} addressed them, saying, “These people are weeping
over us! Then who was it that killed us?”

In another narration, he rebuked them, saying, “You weep and wail for us, yet it
was you who fought against us. Who, then, was it that killed us?” (_Al-Iḥtijāj_)

Even Shia scholars like Jafri confirm that the Shia of Kūfah acknowledged their
betrayal of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} at Karbalāʾ, “We invited the son of
the daughter of our Prophet to come to Kufa to guide us on the right path, but
when he responded to our call we became greedy for our own lives until he was
killed in our midst.” (_The Origins and Early Development of Shi'a Islam_)

Kūfan tears flowed freely after the tragedy, yet those tears could not undo
their betrayal. Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} did not need their weeping after
his martyrdom; he needed their support before it. There is also an important
lesson here for later generations. The reports mention the people of Kūfah
crying, lamenting, and expressing regret, yet neither Zaynab \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}
nor ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} praised them for doing so. Rather,
they condemned their hypocrisy and reminded them that true loyalty is
demonstrated through obedience and steadfastness, not emotional displays after
the fact.

How strange it is, then, that the Shia who claim the deepest love for al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} today continue to engage in practices that neither he nor
the Ahl al-Bayt taught or encouraged. Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} explicitly
instructed his family not to tear their garments, strike themselves, or raise
cries of destruction after his death. Yet centuries later, many of those who
profess devotion to him have turned these very actions into annual rituals.

Ibn Ziyād, may the curse of Allāh be upon him, also sent the severed head of
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} to al-Shām. Before that, however, he openly mocked
the blessed head of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. Anas ibn Mālik
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} reported, “The head of al-Ḥusayn was brought to ʿUbaydullāh
ibn Ziyād and placed in a tray. Ibn Ziyād then began striking the nose and mouth
of al-Ḥusayn with a stick and commenting on his handsome features.”

Anas \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} then said, “Al-Ḥusayn resembled the Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} more than anyone else.” (_Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī_)

Imam Ibn ʿAsākir \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} reports that Ibn Ziyād was only
twenty-eight years old when al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} was martyred. (_Tārīkh
Dimashq_)

In al-Shām, Yazīd allegedly expressed regret and attempted to distance himself
from the actions of his governors in Kūfah. Hishām narrates, “When the head of
al-Ḥusayn was brought before Yazīd, his eyes filled with tears. He said to those
around him, ‘I would have been content with your obedience without the killing
of al-Ḥusayn. May Allāh curse Ibn Sumayyah (ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād). By Allāh,
had I been present, I would have pardoned him.’”

In another report, an emancipated slave of Muʿāwiyah ibn Abī Sufyān
\hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} narrates, “When the head of al-Ḥusayn was placed before
Yazīd, I heard him weeping and saying, ‘Had there been a bond of kinship between
Ibn Ziyād and al-Ḥusayn, he would never have done this.’” (_Al-Bidāyah wa
al-Nihāyah_)

In reality, these were nothing more than crocodile tears, even if true. He
provided provisions and financial assistance to the family of al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} and eventually allowed them to return, after which they made
their way back to Madīnah. This is the least he could do to save his face and
reputation among the Muslims after such a heinous incident occurred under his
command. All narrations portraying Yazīd openly playing with the blessed head of
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, or raping and harming the women of the household
of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_THM}{\BODY}, are false and fabricated.

Even though Yazīd did not explicitly order the killing of al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, he did issue instructions that al-Ḥusayn be resisted and
that his efforts to assume leadership be prevented, even if that resulted in
armed confrontation. Despite expressing words of regret to the family of
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, he took no action against the killers to
demonstrate his disapproval or to avenge the murder, when he should have done
so. On the contrary, there are reports suggesting that he was inwardly pleased
with the overall outcome. The scholars of Ahl al-Sunnah have denounced,
condemned, and in some cases even gone far enough to declare Yazīd a disbeliever
on account of his crimes and conduct.

A man worthy of special mention in these events is al-Mukhtār ibn Abī ʿUbayd
al-Thaqafī. He had previously hosted Muslim ibn ʿAqīl \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} in
Kūfah and was associated with the movement that invited al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} to Iraq. Yet he ultimately failed to aid either Muslim or
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} when they needed support the most.

The very people who had written thousands of letters to al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, invited him to their city, and pledged their support had
abandoned him when he needed them most. Many of them now realized that their
cowardice and betrayal had contributed directly to his martyrdom.

A group of Kūfans therefore formed a movement known as al-Tawwābūn (The
Penitents). They believed that their failure to aid al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} was a grave sin and that sincere repentance required them to
sacrifice themselves fighting those responsible for his death. The movement was
led by the veteran Ṣaḥābī of the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, Sulaymān ibn Ṣurad
al-Khuzāʿī \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, who had been among those who invited al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} to Kūfah but failed to support him when the time came.

Following the death of Yazīd in 64 AH, the Tawwābūn openly called the people to
repentance and gathered thousands of supporters. Before setting out, they
travelled to Karbalāʾ, wept at the grave of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY},
renewed their pledge of repentance, and vowed to fight until death. Their
objective was not political power but expiation for their betrayal. In 65 AH
they marched north and confronted the Umayyad forces at the Battle of ʿAyn
al-Wardah. Although they fought courageously, they were heavily outnumbered and
suffered a devastating defeat. Sulaymān ibn Ṣurad and most of the movement's
leaders were killed, effectively bringing the Tawwābūn movement to an end.
(_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_)

The death of Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiyah in 64 AH had plunged the Muslim world into
further political turmoil. During this period, ʿAbdullāh ibn al-Zubayr
\hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} established his authority over much of the Muslim world from
Makkah and was recognized as Khalīfah across large regions of the Islamic lands.
Meanwhile, the Umayyad state entered a period of instability and internal
conflict.

After the death of Yazīd, seeking to rally the people around himself, al-Mukhtār
began calling them towards Muḥammad ibn al-Ḥanafīyah \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, the
son of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. He portrayed Muḥammad ibn
al-Ḥanafīyah as the promised Mahdī and used the slogan of avenging the blood of
al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} to attract followers. Over time, however,
al-Mukhtār's claims became increasingly extreme. He alleged that revelation
descended upon him and that the angel Jibrīl brought him messages. For this
reason, the scholars of Islam regarded him as one of the greatest liars to
emerge within this Ummah.

Indeed, the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} foretold that from the tribe
of Thaqīf would emerge a great liar and a great destroyer. The scholars
identified the liar as al-Mukhtār ibn Abī ʿUbayd and the destroyer as al-Ḥajjāj
ibn Yūsuf. (_Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim_)

Al-Mukhtār eventually seized control of Kūfah and established a movement that
outwardly revolved around avenging Karbalāʾ. Whatever his true motives may have
been, his forces succeeded in pursuing many of those who had directly
participated in the murder of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. Among those killed
by his followers were Shimr ibn Dhī al-Jawshan, Sinān ibn Anas, and later
ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād himself.

The movement of al-Mukhtār continued for several years until ʿAbdullāh ibn
al-Zubayr \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} ordered his brother, the distinguished commander
Muṣʿab ibn al-Zubayr \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, to confront him. Muṣʿab marched
against Kūfah, defeated al-Mukhtār's forces, and killed him in 67 AH, bringing
an end to one of the most dangerous and deceptive movements of the first century
of Islam. (_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_)

Imam al-Tirmidhī \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY} narrated in his _Jāmiʿ_ that ʿUmārah ibn
ʿUmayr said, “When the heads of ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād and his men were brought
and placed in the hall of the mosque, I reached them while the people were
saying, ‘It comes! It comes!’ A snake then slithered through the heads until it
entered the nostrils of ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād, where it remained for a short
while before leaving. It then disappeared. The people again said, ‘It comes! It
comes!’ and it repeated this two or three times.” (_Jāmiʿ al-Tirmidhī_) Thus, the
very man who orchestrated the murder of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} did not
escape the punishment of Allāh in this world before facing the punishment of the
Hereafter.

Imam Ibn Kathīr records from Abū Rajāʾ al-ʿUṭāridī, “There was a man from our
neighbors who participated in the killing of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. He
would say, ‘Look at this man whom Allāh killed.’ Abū Rajāʾ said, I replied to
him, ‘Rather, Allāh killed you.’ Thereafter the man became blind.” (_Al-Bidāyah
wa al-Nihāyah_)

The closest people to Allāh are those who are tested the most. Saʿd ibn Abī
Waqqāṣ \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} reported, “I said, ‘O Messenger of Allāh, which
people are tested most severely?’ The Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}
said, ‘The Prophets, then those nearest to them, then those nearest to them. A
man is tested according to his religion. If he is firm in his religion, his
trials are increased. If there is weakness in his religion, he is tested
according to the strength of his religion. The servant continues to be tested
until he walks upon the earth free of sin.’” (_Jāmiʿ al-Tirmidhī_)

Allāh honored al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} in this world by making him
the beloved grandson of the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, the son of
Fāṭimah al-Zahrāʾ \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}, and one of the most beloved members of
the Ahl al-Bayt. He honored him further by granting him martyrdom while standing
upon truth, patience, courage, and dignity. As for the Hereafter, the Messenger
of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} testified regarding al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}, “Al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn are the leaders of the youth of
Jannah.” (_Jāmiʿ al-Tirmidhī_)

The tragedy of Karbalāʾ remains one of the most painful events in Islamic
history. Every Muslim grieves the martyrdom of al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY},
loves the Ahl al-Bayt, and curses those who betrayed, abandoned, and murdered
him. Yet true love for al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} is not expressed through
emotion alone. Rather, it is expressed by following the Quran and Sunnah that he
lived for, defended, and ultimately gave his life to uphold. May Allāh be
pleased with al-Ḥusayn, his family, and his companions, and may He gather them
with their grandfather Muḥammad \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} in the highest levels of
Jannah.

Over the centuries, numerous beliefs, customs, rituals, and narratives became
attached to the events of Karbalāʾ. Some were rooted in authentic teachings,
while many others were later innovations that neither al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} nor the early generations of Islam ever practiced or
approved of. Having examined what actually occurred at Karbalāʾ, it is now
necessary to examine some of the practices and beliefs that later emerged in its
aftermath.
