
# Who Are al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn?

Al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} were the beloved grandsons of the
Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, the sons of ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib and
Fāṭimah al-Zahrāʾ \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}, and among the most virtuous members of
Ahl al-Bayt. Al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} occupy a unique
and lofty position in the history of Islam. They were not only the beloved
grandsons of the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, but were also nurtured
directly under his guidance, character, and example. To study their lives is to
gain insight into the Prophetic household itself and to witness the practical
embodiment of the values taught by the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}.
The Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} loved them immensely and instructed the Ummah to
honor and love his family, saying regarding them, "Al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn are
the leaders of the youth of Paradise." (_Jāmiʿ al-Tirmidhī_) Learning about them
is therefore not a matter of historical curiosity alone; it is a means of
drawing closer to Allāh through loving those whom His Messenger
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} loved.

In every generation, people have spoken much about al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}, yet true love is not merely expressed through words,
emotions, or claims of affiliation. Rather, true love is manifested through
knowledge, respect, imitation, and adherence to their example. Their lives teach
us patience in times of trial, steadfastness upon truth, dignity in leadership,
devotion in worship, generosity in conduct, and sincerity in serving the
religion of Allāh. Al-Ḥasan \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} demonstrated the virtue of
reconciliation and unity when he relinquished authority for the greater good of
the Muslims, while al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} embodied courage, sacrifice,
and steadfastness in the face of oppression. Together, they represent two of the
greatest models of Prophetic character after the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}.

For this reason, every Muslim should strive to know their biographies, virtues,
struggles, and contributions to Islam. Through understanding their lives, one
develops a deeper appreciation of Ahl al-Bayt, a greater love for the Companions
and the early generations, and a clearer understanding of the events that shaped
the history of this Ummah. A proper understanding of their lives is also
essential for understanding one of the most significant and painful events in
Islamic history: the tragedy of Karbalāʾ. Far too often, this event is studied
in isolation from the lives, virtues, upbringing, and contributions of those who
were at its centre. Before delving into the incident of Karbalāʾ and the events
that led to it, it is important to first become acquainted with the lives of
these two noble grandsons of the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}.

All the libraries of the world and all the ink of mankind would fall short if
one were to attempt to fully describe the virtues and lofty rank of the beloved
grandsons of the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}, al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}. What follows, therefore, is a brief attempt at summarizing
the lives of our masters al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn, the sons of ʿAlī
\hon{\P_THM}{\BODY} for our readers.

## Al-Ḥasan ibn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib \hon{\P_TWO}{\HEADER}

Abū Muḥammad al-Ḥasan ibn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib
\hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} was the elder grandson of the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} and one of the most noble members of Ahl al-Bayt. He was born
in Madīnah in Ramaḍān 3 AH. (_Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrá_) The Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} performed _taḥnīk_ for him and named him al-Ḥasan after ʿAlī
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} had intended to name him Ḥarb. (Musnad Aḥmad) He became
known as Sibṭ Rasūlillāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} (the grandson of the Messenger of
Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}) and Rayḥānat al-Nabī \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} (the sweet
basil of the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}), and was among those who most closely
resembled the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} in appearance. (_Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī_)

ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} said, “Al-Ḥasan resembles the Messenger
of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} from the chest upwards, while al-Ḥusayn resembles
him from the chest downwards.” (_Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī_)

Al-Barāʾ ibn ʿĀzib \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} narrated that he saw the Messenger of
Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} carrying al-Ḥasan on his shoulders and saying, "O
Allāh, I love him, so love him." (_Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī_)

Al-Ḥasan \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} was approximately seven years old when the Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} passed away. (_Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrá_) During the Khilāfah of
ʿUmar ibn al-Khaṭṭāb \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, he and his brother al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} were granted stipends equal to those of the veterans of
Badr, namely five thousand dirhams annually. (_Kitāb al-Amwāl_)

He \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} participated in a number of military expeditions during
the Khilāfahs of ʿUthmān and Muʿāwiyah \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}, including campaigns
in Ṭarābulus (Tripoli, Libya), other regions of North Africa, Khurāsān (parts of
present-day Iran, Afghanistan, and Turkmenistan), Ṭabaristān along the southern
coast of the Caspian Sea in present-day northern Iran, and Jurjān (Gurgān in
present-day northeastern Iran), among other expeditions. (_Al-Bidāyah wa
al-Nihāyah_)

The generosity of al-Ḥasan ibn ʿAlī \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} became proverbial. It is
reported that he gave away all of his wealth for the sake of Allāh on three
separate occasions, and on two further occasions divided his entire wealth
equally with the poor, keeping only half for himself. (_Ḥilyat al-Awliyāʾ_)

During the siege of ʿUthmān \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, he was among those sent by his
father ʿAlī \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} to defend the Khalīfah and is reported to have
sustained injuries in the process. Following the martyrdom of ʿAlī
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} in Ramaḍān 40 AH, al-Ḥasan \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} supervised
his _ghusl_ and burial. (_Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrā_)

The Muslims pledged allegiance to him as Khalīfah. His Khilāfah lasted for
approximately six months before he voluntarily relinquished authority to
Muʿāwiyah ibn Abī Sufyān \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} in 41 AH, thereby bringing an end
to a period of division and bloodshed among the Muslims. In doing so, he
fulfilled the Prophet\'s \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} prophecy, "Indeed, this son of mine
is a leader, and perhaps Allāh will reconcile through him two great groups of
Muslims." (_Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī_)

The historians report that he performed Ḥajj on foot numerous times, some
reports mentioning twenty-five occasions. (_Siyar Aʿlām al-Nubalāʾ_) He was
renowned for his generosity and would frequently distribute large portions of
his wealth in charity.

Most historians report that al-Ḥasan \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} died after being
poisoned, although the identity of the perpetrator remains disputed.
(_Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrá_) He passed away in Madīnah in 49 or 50 AH at
approximately forty-seven years of age. (_Al-Iṣābah_) His _janāzah_ prayer was
led by Saʿīd ibn al-ʿĀṣ \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}, the Governor of Madīnah, and he was
buried in al-Baqīʿ near his grandmother Fāṭimah bint Asad \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}.
(_Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrá_)

He married a number of women during his lifetime, though the exact number is
disputed among historians and genealogists. He was blessed with numerous
children, and the genealogists mention that he had twelve sons and five
daughters. The progeny of al-Ḥasan \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} continued primarily
through al-Ḥasan al-Muthannā and Zayd ibn al-Ḥasan. (_Ansāb al-Ashrāf_) Among
his children were sons named Abū Bakr, ʿUmar, Zayd, Ḥamzah, and Ṭalḥah (_Usd
al-Ghābah_), reflecting the affection, respect, and close ties that existed
between Ahl al-Bayt and the noble Ṣaḥābah \hon{\P_THM}{\BODY}. Three of his
sons, ʿAbdullāh, al-Qāsim, and Abū Bakr, were later martyred at Karbalāʾ
alongside their uncle al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. Al-Ḥasan
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} narrated thirteen aḥādīth from the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}. (_Al-Iṣābah_)

## Al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib \hon{\P_TWO}{\HEADER}

Abū ʿAbdillāh al-Ḥusayn ibn ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib ibn ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib
\hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY} was the younger grandson of the Messenger of Allāh
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} and one of the most distinguished members of Ahl al-Bayt. He
was born in Madīnah in Shaʿbān of 4 AH. (_Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrá_) The Prophet
\hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} performed _taḥnīk_ for him and named him al-Ḥusayn after ʿAlī
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} had intended to name him Ḥarb. (_Musnad Aḥmad_) He became
known as Sibṭ Rasūlillāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} (the grandson of the Messenger of
Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}) and Rayḥānat al-Nabī \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY} (the sweet
basil of the Prophet \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}). (_Siyar Aʿlām al-Nubalāʾ_)

He was breastfed by Umm al-Faḍl Lubābah bint al-Ḥārith \hon{\P_HER}{\BODY}
alongside her son Qutham ibn al-ʿAbbās \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. (_Usd al-Ghābah_) He
was approximately six years old when the Messenger of Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}
passed away. (_Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrá_)

He \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} participated in a number of military expeditions during
the Khilāfahs of ʿUthmān and Muʿāwiyah \hon{\P_TWO}{\BODY}, including campaigns
in Ṭarābulus (Tripoli, Libya), other regions of North Africa, Khurāsān (parts of
present-day Iran, Afghanistan, and Turkmenistan), Ṭabaristān along the southern
coast of the Caspian Sea in present-day northern Iran, and Jurjān (Gurgān in
present-day northeastern Iran), among other expeditions (_Tārīkh al-Islām_). He
is also reported to have participated in the expedition against Constantinople
during the Khilāfah of Muʿāwiyah \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY}. (_Al-Bidāyah wa
al-Nihāyah_)

He was known for his knowledge, worship, generosity, and teaching, and would
conduct gatherings of learning in Masjid al-Nabawī where students and seekers of
knowledge benefited from him. (_Ḥilyat al-Awliyāʾ_) It is reported that he
performed Ḥajj on foot from Madīnah to Makkah on numerous occasions, some
reports mentioning twenty-five times, and that he would personally serve
pilgrims and assist them, seeking no distinction over others. (_Siyar Aʿlām
al-Nubalāʾ_)

Following the death of Muʿāwiyah \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} in 60 AH, al-Ḥusayn
\hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} declined to pledge allegiance to Yazīd ibn Muʿāwiyah and
departed for Makkah. There he received numerous letters from the people of Kūfah
pledging their support and inviting him to come to Iraq. (_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_)
However, after he set out toward Kūfah, the situation changed dramatically, and
those who had promised support failed to stand by him. He was eventually
surrounded at Karbalāʾ along with a small group of family members and
companions. On the 10th of Muḥarram 61 AH, he was martyred after displaying
extraordinary courage, patience, and steadfastness upon the truth. (_Tārīkh
al-Islām_)

Many members of Ahl al-Bayt were martyred alongside him, including his sons,
nephews, brothers, and cousins. Among the adult male descendants of al-Ḥusayn,
only ʿAlī ibn al-Ḥusayn Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn survived, having been too ill to
participate in the fighting. (_Al-Ṭabaqāt al-Kubrá_) His blessed head was
severed after his martyrdom and presented to ʿUbaydullāh ibn Ziyād in Kūfah.
(_Tārīkh al-Ṭabarī_) The subsequent location and burial of the blessed head
became a matter of historical disagreement among scholars and historians,
with several opinions being reported. (_Siyar Aʿlām al-Nubalāʾ_)

Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} married several times and was blessed with
children. Genealogists mention that he had four sons and two daughters. His
lineage continued through ʿAlī Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn \hon{\M_HIM}{\BODY}, from whom
the majority of the descendants of al-Ḥusayn trace their ancestry. (_Ansāb
al-Ashrāf_) When Zayn al-ʿĀbidīn returned to Madīnah, he benefited from and
narrated from senior Companions such as Jābir ibn ʿAbdillāh, ʿAbdullāh ibn
ʿAbbās, ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿUmar, and Abū Hurayrah \hon{\P_THM}{\BODY}. (_Siyar Aʿlām
al-Nubalā_)

Al-Ḥusayn \hon{\P_HIM}{\BODY} narrated eight _aḥādīth_ from the Messenger of
Allāh \hon{\PBUH}{\BODY}. (_Al-Iṣābah_). His life remains a timeless example of
courage, sacrifice, devotion, honor, and steadfast adherence to principle, and
his martyrdom at Karbalāʾ remains one of the most significant and painful events
in the history of the Muslim Ummah.
